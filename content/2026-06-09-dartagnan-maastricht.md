# The D'Artagnan Skeleton: A 350-Year Mystery in Maastricht

In February 2026, repair workers at the **Sint-Petrus-en-Pauluskerk** in **Wolder** — a district of Maastricht — discovered something extraordinary beneath the church floor. A skeleton, buried under what had been the altar two centuries earlier, lay alongside a French coin and a musket ball lodged in the chest area.

The remains might belong to **Charles de Batz de Castelmore, Count d'Artagnan** — the real-life captain of Louis XIV's musketeers, immortalised by Alexandre Dumas in *The Three Musketeers*.

---

## The Siege of Maastricht, 1673

D'Artagnan died on **25 June 1673** during the French siege of Maastricht, part of the Franco-Dutch War. Leading a regiment of musketeers in a rear assault, he managed to penetrate the fortifications but was struck in the throat by a musket ball. City records describe sergeant Saint-Léger finding him dead with his throat shattered.

The French army had camped near the Wolder church. The assumption, never proven, was that d'Artagnan was buried there. For 350 years, his final resting place remained a mystery.

---

## The Discovery

When part of the church floor collapsed in early 2026, workers found the skeleton alongside:

- A **French coin**
- A **musket ball** in the chest area

The body was buried beneath the old altar — a location historically reserved for royalty and important figures. Diaken Jos Valke noted: *"Only royalty or other important figures were buried under the altar."*

DNA from the teeth was sent to a German laboratory for comparison with a descendant of the De Batz family in Avignon. Results are expected soon.

---

## The Controversy

But the story took a dark turn. The excavation was carried out by **Wim Dijkman**, a retired city archaeologist, **without permits or professional oversight**.

The Sint-Petrus-en-Pauluskerk has been a **protected national monument since 1966**. Dutch heritage law requires formal approval for any ground disturbance. None was obtained.

What followed was described by involved experts as catastrophic:

> *"He was literally standing on top of the skeleton... Archaeologists normally clear a large area around the remains. But Dijkman did not do that."*

- Bone material was **disturbed with bare hands**
- Fragments were **thrown together in plastic ice cream containers inside Albert Heijn bags**
- **50–80% of the skeleton** is now considered compromised
- Bones from **other individuals** (including young people) were mixed into the remains
- Researchers can no longer reliably distinguish DNA from the bones versus from the excavator

---

## The Fallout

The municipality of Maastricht declared the dig **illegal** and reported it to the Dutch Heritage Inspectorate. Dijkman was **briefly arrested** after refusing to surrender an arm bone and two teeth. He had sent them to a Munich lab for DNA analysis and claimed returning them by post was too risky.

In a cruel irony, Dijkman received a **royal knighthood** in April 2026 as the "discoverer" of the remains — weeks before his arrest.

Diaken Valke, who initially believed the find was genuine, described watching Dijkman work as like *"watching a surgeon with a butter knife."* Yet Valke remains **"99.9% certain"** the bones belong to d'Artagnan.

---

## What Happens Next

The municipality will present **first identification results on 17 June 2026**, but has already warned they will **likely not be conclusive** due to the damage.

If DNA confirmation does come, it would be — as historian Julien Wilmart noted — *"the first time in history that a fictional hero has somehow become real."*

For now, the mystery endures. And the evidence that might have solved it may have been destroyed by the very people who sought to uncover it.

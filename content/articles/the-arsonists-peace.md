# The Arsonist's Peace: How Trump Declared Victory Over a Fire He Lit

On his eightieth birthday, Donald Trump gave himself the only present that ever mattered to him: a headline.

"The Deal with the Islamic Republic of Iran is now complete," he wrote on Truth Social. "Congratulations to all!" Then came the flourish that would be clipped into every news package: "Many presidents have tried to make Peace with Iran, and all have failed before me."

It was vintage Trump — the builder of the fire claiming credit for the fire brigade. Because the war he now claims to have ended? He started it.

---

## The War That Shouldn't Have Needed Ending

On 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel launched coordinated strikes on Iran — Operation Epic Fury and Operation Roaring Lion — targeting military installations, nuclear sites, and government infrastructure across the country. The Institute for the Study of War described the objective bluntly: to "topple the Islamic Republic."

Trump didn't describe it that way in public, but he didn't hide much either. In early March, he declared that Iran's only path to peace was "unconditional surrender." He told the world he intended to "annihilate" Iran's military capabilities, abolish its nuclear program, and liberate its people. He encouraged Iranians to rise up and overthrow their government.

Three months later, none of that happened. And Trump has adjusted accordingly.

"As far as regime change, I never cared about regime change," he told The Wall Street Journal on 14 June. "This is the third group we've dealt with, and this is the most rational group yet."

The annihilator became the negotiator.

---

## What the Deal Actually Does

The framework agreement, mediated by Pakistan and backed by Saudi Arabia, Turkey, Egypt, and Qatar, does accomplish some things:

- It halts military operations on all fronts.
- It reopens the Strait of Hormuz, the vital shipping lane whose closure had rattled global energy markets and driven up food and fuel prices worldwide.
- It provides for the phased lifting of sanctions and the release of approximately $24 billion in frozen Iranian assets.
- It establishes a two-month window for nuclear negotiations.

These are not nothing. Global oil prices fell more than $3 a barrel on the announcement. Asian stock markets gained over 5%.

But here's what the deal does not do:

- It does not dismantle Iran's nuclear program. The hardest questions — who takes Iran's enriched uranium, whether enrichment continues, what inspections look like — are punted into the future.
- It does not topple Iran's theocratic leadership. The regime Trump aimed to annihilate remains firmly in power.
- It does not compel Iran's capitulation. Iran retains its enriched uranium, its military, and its government.

In other words, the deal restores the pre-war status quo — the same status quo that existed before Trump and Netanyahu launched their strikes in February. The Strait of Hormuz, which had been open before the war, will be open again. The sanctions, which had been in place before the war, may be lifted. Iran's nuclear program, which had been contained under the JCPOA before Trump unilaterally withdrew from it in 2018, will now be negotiated again.

The president who called the JCPOA "the worst deal ever negotiated" has now negotiated a deal that gets him back to roughly where the JCPOA left things.

---

## The Uninvited Guest

There's another problem with Trump's victory lap: the deal's most important regional player isn't party to it.

Israel is not a signatory. Benjamin Netanyahu has made clear that his government has no intention of withdrawing from Lebanon, where Israeli forces have been battling Hebollah since early March. Israeli Defense Minister Israel Katz warned that Israel "could still act independently toward Iran" and would not pull out of occupied zones in Lebanon, Syria, or Gaza.

Netanyahu and Trump are "in full agreement" that Iran must not have nuclear weapons, the Israeli prime minister said. But the deal doesn't guarantee that outcome. It merely creates a deadline to discuss it. In the meantime, Iran still has enriched uranium, and Israel still has its finger on the trigger.

The president who claims to have brought peace to the Middle East has in fact brokered a bilateral ceasefire with Iran while his ally continues fighting on another front.

---

## The Performer at Work

None of this is accidental. The Trump playbook has always depended on a particular alchemy: create a crisis, escalate it to maximum drama, then claim credit for any de-escalation.

In this case, the escalation was literal. On 11 June — just three days before announcing the deal — Trump threatened to attack Iran "VERY HARD TONIGHT" and to seize its oil and gas infrastructure. He threatened to take over Kharg Island. He raised the specter of total economic destruction.

Then he announced a deal.

The International Crisis Group's Ali Vaez described the pattern accurately: "Trump is using the threat either to force the Iranians to make last-minute concessions, or to create the appearance of having extracted concessions through pressure."

Either way, the performance worked. The headlines wrote themselves. "Trump Announces Peace Deal." "Let the Oil Flow!" "Many Presidents Failed Before Me."

The details — an unresolved nuclear program, an intact regime, an unbound ally — are for analysts. The headline is what matters.

---

## The Birthday Present

There's a grim symmetry to the timing. Trump announced the deal on his eightieth birthday — a milestone that no other American president has reached in office. The coincidence was too perfect to ignore. After months of threatening, negotiating, and threatening again, he could close the weekend with a self-made gift: proof that he was still the dealmaker, still the closer, still the man who succeeded where others failed.

Never mind that those "others" never started a war with Iran.

Never mind that Barack Obama's Iran nuclear deal — which Trump tore up in 2018 — had achieved more non-proliferation in its first year than this framework promises in its first sixty days.

Never mind that the "peace" Trump celebrates is merely a cessation of the hostilities he initiated.

"Ships of the World, start your engines," he wrote. "Let the oil flow!"

The oil had been flowing before he stopped it. The fire had not been burning before he lit it. And the arsonist will be remembered for putting out his own fire — until the next crisis demands his attention, and the cycle begins again.

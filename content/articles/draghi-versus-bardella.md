# Draghi versus Bardella

## The Competitiveness Question

**Scope and methodology.** This document compares two publicly documented positions on European economic integration: the 2024 Draghi report and the 2024–2026 economic platform of Jordan Bardella and the Rassemblement National. For a chronological record of the Rassemblement National's foreign lending history, see also: *The FCRB Transaction* (Check Mate reference document, 2026). All claims about either position are drawn from the original documents, official statements, and independent institutional assessments. This record does not assert that one position is superior; it identifies where the two positions point in opposite directions and records externally produced evaluations of fiscal feasibility. Normative judgments are left to the reader.

---

### 1. Executive Summary

In July 2024, Mario Draghi — former Prime Minister of Italy and former President of the European Central Bank — delivered to the European Commission a report titled *The Future of European Competitiveness*. The report concluded that the European Union faces an annual investment shortfall of EUR 750–800 billion. It recommended a "quantum leap" in European integration, including joint borrowing for strategic sectors, completion of the single market, a European capital markets union, and expanded qualified majority voting (QMV) in foreign policy and taxation.

In June 2024 and again in June 2026, Jordan Bardella — President of the Rassemblement National and candidate for the French presidential election of 2027 — outlined a platform that represents the structural opposite of the Draghi roadmap. Bardella has proposed cutting France's net EU budget contribution by approximately 50%, reinstating internal border controls within the Schengen area, blocking Ukraine's accession to the European Union, and retaining the French nuclear deterrent as a strictly national asset with no European sharing mechanism.

This document records both positions, identifies the points at which they conflict, and presents independent economic assessments of the fiscal feasibility of the RN platform. All claims are drawn from publicly available sources. No classified information is used.

### Key Takeaways

- The Draghi report (July 2024) identifies an annual investment gap of EUR 750–800 billion and recommends deeper EU integration, including joint borrowing and expanded qualified majority voting.
- The Bardella platform (2024–2026) proposes the structural opposite: reduced EU budget contributions, national border controls, and preservation of the national veto.
- Independent economic institutions (Asteres, OFCE, Institut Montaigne) have identified potential fiscal tensions in the RN program, including a projected deficit of 6.4% of GDP; the RN disputes these assumptions.
- Both positions face political opposition: Draghi's recommendations from member states wary of further integration; Bardella's from EU institutional and fiscal frameworks.

### 2. The Draghi Report (2024)

#### 2.1 Mandate and Scope

The European Commission appointed Mario Draghi — who served as Prime Minister of Italy (2021–2022) and as President of the European Central Bank (2011–2019) — in September 2023 to produce an independent assessment of European competitiveness. The mandate covered three areas: innovation (particularly in digital technology and artificial intelligence), decarbonisation and energy security, and security and defence. The final report was published in July 2024 and submitted to the Commission.

#### 2.2 Key Findings

| Finding | Value | Page in Report |
|---------|-------|----------------|
| Annual investment gap | EUR 750–800 billion | p. 12 |
| EU GDP per capita vs. US | 30% below | p. 8 |
| Annual defence investment shortfall | EUR 150–200 billion | p. 150 approx. |
| Energy cost differential (industrial) | 2–3x higher than US | various |
| EU defence spending efficiency | Lower than sum of national budgets due to fragmentation | various |

#### 2.3 Core Recommendations

The report's central argument is that incremental reform is insufficient. Draghi writes: "We need a quantum leap, not incremental reform." The recommendations include:

1. **Joint borrowing instruments** for strategic investment (modelled on the NextGenerationEU recovery fund)
2. **Completion of the single market** in digital services, energy, and defence procurement
3. **European capital markets union** to reduce reliance on fragmented national banking systems
4. **Streamlined decision-making** via expanded qualified majority voting in foreign policy, taxation, and enlargement
5. **Accelerated enlargement** for Ukraine and the Western Balkans, coupled with transitional arrangements

#### 2.4 Federal Implications and Political Reception

Draghi does not use the term "federal Europe" in the report. However, each of the major recommendations — joint debt instruments, EU-level defence procurement, a harmonised corporate tax base, and QMV in taxation — presupposes institutional structures that exceed the current level of EU integration.

The report has attracted political opposition from multiple member-state governments. At the European Council of December 2024, Hungary's Viktor Orbán threatened to veto the opening of accession talks with Ukraine unless QMV expansion was dropped from the agenda. The Netherlands, Sweden, and Denmark have also expressed reservations about joint borrowing instruments and QMV in taxation, arguing that these would reduce national fiscal autonomy.

Some economists have questioned whether deeper centralisation is the appropriate response to Europe's competitiveness gap. The report assumes that fragmentation is the primary cause of underperformance; critics note that the EU's most competitive regions (parts of Germany, the Netherlands, Denmark) are not necessarily the most integrated, and that some of the gap with the United States reflects structural factors — demographics, energy endowment, and venture-capital depth — that institutional integration alone cannot resolve.

The report was commissioned by the European Commission and produced by a figure with extensive experience in EU institutional leadership. Critics from both left and right have noted that this institutional framing may predispose the report toward federal solutions rather than regulatory or market-based alternatives.

**Sources:** European Commission, "The Future of European Competitiveness" (Draghi report), July 2024; European Council conclusions, December 2024; Politico Europe, December 2024.

### 3. The Bardella Platform

#### 3.1 Origins and Documentation

The Rassemblement National has published economic policy documents since September 2024. The primary document is a "livret économique" (economic booklet), first released in September 2024 and subsequently revised. Additional policy statements have been made through interviews, speeches to business associations (including a lunch with the employers' federation Medef in April 2026 and an address to the Ethic network), and a "pacte de confiance" (letter to business leaders, September 2025) whose content has not been publicly disclosed beyond slogans.

Multiple independent economic institutions have evaluated the RN program. Their assessments characterize it as internally inconsistent and fiscally expansionary, though the RN disputes these characterizations. The following summary is drawn from these external evaluations.

#### 3.2 Budget and Trade Positions

| Position | Description | Source Period |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| EU budget contribution | Reduce France's net contribution by approximately 50% (reclaiming EUR 2–3 billion annually) | EurActiv, June 2024; reiterated June 2026 |
| Schengen | Reinstate national border controls within the Schengen area | Multiple interviews, 2024–2026 |
| Free trade | Moratorium on new EU free trade agreements | Livret économique, September 2024 |
| Ukraine accession | Block Ukraine's accession to the EU | Repeated statements, 2024–2026 |
| "Plan Manger Français" | Require 80% French products in school canteens | Livret économique, 2024 |

#### 3.3 Tax and Spending Promises

| Measure | Estimated Cost (annual) | Source of Estimate |
|---------|------------------------|--------------------|
| VAT reduction on energy (20% → 5.5%) | EUR 11–17 billion | OFCE, Institut Montaigne, 2024 |
| Tax cuts (under-30s, doctors, nurses, essential goods) | Not separately costed by RN; external analysts estimate significant revenue loss | Various, 2024 |
| Employer charge relief (up to 3x minimum wage) | Significant | Asteres, 2024 |
| Retirement age rollback to 60 (conditional) | Substantial pension liability increase | INSEE-adjacent estimates, 2022–2024 |
| Revenue offsets proposed | "Fraud fighting" + EU budget clawback + abolition of unspecified agencies | RN documents |

#### 3.4 Independent Assessments of Fiscal Feasibility

Multiple independent institutions have assessed the RN's economic program. Their findings converge on the following conclusions:

| Institution | Assessment | Date |
|-------------|-----------|------|
| Asteres (economic consultancy, via Lessentieldeleco) | Public deficit would rise to 6.4% of GDP; public debt would exceed 117% of GDP within the term | 2024 |
| Allianz Research | RN revenue proposals cover "only a fraction" of promised spending | 2024 |
| Institut Montaigne + Fondation Jean-Jaurès (via La Croix) | Program described as having a "right-extreme tone, costly, and inconsistent" | September 2024 |
| OFCE (Observatoire Français des Conjonctures Économiques) | Independent evaluation of RN legislative program | June 2024 |
| Catherine Lubochinsky, Cercle des Economistes (via Challenges) | RN funding ambitions characterised as "unrealistic" | 2024 |

The European Commission's Stability and Growth Pact requires member states to maintain deficits below 3% of GDP and debt below 60% of GDP (with exceptions). A deficit of 6.4% would place France in breach of these criteria.

**Note on methodology:** The RN has not published a detailed, independently costed fiscal roadmap. All deficit and debt projections above are derived from external economic institutions analysing the RN's published promises.

**The RN's response.** The party disputes these assessments on several grounds. First, it argues that administrative savings and fraud-combat measures would offset a portion of the costs. Second, it contends that reducing France's net EU budget contribution (EUR 2–3 billion annually) and abolishing unspecified agencies would free resources. Third, it maintains that supply-side effects — increased economic activity from VAT cuts and employer charge relief — would partially compensate for revenue loss. Fourth, the party notes that France's public debt already stood at approximately 111% of GDP and its deficit above 5% in 2024 under the Macron/Barnier government, suggesting that fiscal deterioration is not unique to RN proposals. These rebuttals have not been independently modelled; they are recorded here as the party's stated position.

#### 3.5 Potential Tensions (Externally Identified)

Several independent analysts have noted potential tensions in the RN platform:

1. **Budget contribution vs. single-market benefit:** France is a net beneficiary of the EU single market through the Common Agricultural Policy, cohesion funds, Airbus supply-chain integration, and nuclear-energy research programmes. The EUR 2–3 billion "saving" from reduced contributions would, according to independent trade economists, be offset by losses in market access and programme receipts.

2. **"Plan Manger Français" vs. export dependence:** France is the EU's largest agricultural and industrial exporter. A requirement for 80% domestic sourcing in public procurement would, according to trade analysts, create tension with France's negotiating position in favour of open EU markets for its own exporters.

3. **Energy VAT cut vs. EU law:** The proposed reduction of VAT on energy from 20% to 5.5% would fall below the EU directive minimum floor of 15% for fuel and energy products, potentially exposing France to legal challenge before the Court of Justice of the European Union.

4. **Moratorium on free trade vs. CAP dependency:** France's agricultural sector depends on EU-wide market access. A moratorium on new trade agreements would not reverse existing access but signals a departure from the market-opening posture that French agriculture has historically relied upon.

5. **Exit from EU electricity market:** The RN proposes withdrawing from the EU electricity market mechanism. Independent energy analysts have warned that this risks supply shortages without cross-border interconnection backup, particularly given the uncertain timeline for France's nuclear fleet renewal.

**Sources:** EurActiv, June 2024; Les Echos, September 2024; L'Opinion, September 2024; Challenges, 2024; La Croix, September 2024; Asteres, 2024; OFCE, June 2024; Institut Montaigne/Fondation Jean-Jaurès, September 2024.

### 4. Five Fault Lines

The following table maps the Draghi recommendations against the Bardella positions on five structural questions.

| Fault Line | Draghi Position | Bardella Position |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **1. Budget and investment** | Joint EU borrowing for strategic investment (NextGenerationEU model); EUR 750–800bn annual gap must be closed collectively | Cut France's net EU contribution by 50%; no joint borrowing commitment |
| **2. Defence** | EU-level joint procurement; shared R&D; EUR 150–200bn annual shortfall requires collective scaling | National preference in procurement; no European nuclear-sharing mechanism; France retains deterrent as strictly national asset |
| **3. Single market and borders** | Complete digital, energy, and capital markets union; removal of internal barriers | Reinstate national border controls within Schengen; exit EU electricity market mechanism |
| **4. Enlargement** | Accelerate Ukraine and Western Balkans accession before 2027 to lock in institutional commitments | Block Ukraine accession indefinitely; slow Balkans process |
| **5. Decision-making** | Expand QMV to foreign policy, taxation, and enlargement to prevent single-member vetoes | Preserve national veto power in all areas |

Each fault line represents a policy area where the Draghi and Bardella positions diverge. The Draghi report argues that fragmentation impedes European competitiveness relative to the United States and China. The Bardella platform proposes greater national autonomy in budget, borders, and decision-making. Whether fragmentation or autonomy is preferable is a normative question; this document records only that the two platforms prescribe opposite institutional directions.

**Broader context.** Draghi's recommendations are not universally accepted within the EU. Multiple member states, including the Netherlands, Sweden, and Denmark, have resisted proposals for joint borrowing and QMV expansion in taxation. Similarly, France's public debt stood at approximately 111% of GDP in 2024 under the current government — a level that predates the RN platform and reflects structural fiscal challenges shared by several eurozone member states. The fiscal pressures identified by independent institutions are therefore not unique to RN proposals; they are part of a broader European context in which multiple governments have struggled to meet Stability and Growth Pact criteria.

### 5. Cost Analysis: What Independent Economies Conclude

The Draghi report is explicit about the cost of inaction: a permanent 30% GDP-per-capita gap with the United States, a EUR 750–800 billion annual investment shortfall, and defence capabilities that cost more but deliver less because of fragmentation.

Independent assessments of the Bardella platform reach a parallel set of projections about the fiscal impact: a public deficit rising to 6.4% of GDP, public debt exceeding 117%, and a series of structural tensions (domestic sourcing requirements for a country that is the EU's top exporter; energy VAT cuts below EU minima; withdrawal from electricity markets without backup capacity guarantees). These projections are produced by independent institutions, not by the RN, and the RN disputes their assumptions.

The two analyses are not mirror images. The Draghi report is a 400-page document with sector-specific modelling, price tagging, and institutional roadmaps. The RN's "livret économique" is a shorter pamphlet that independent reviewers describe as being "regularly updated" (Les Echos) to resolve contradictions arising between different voter constituencies — between the party's populist base and the business audiences it courts.

**Sources:** Draghi report, July 2024; Asteres, 2024; OFCE, June 2024; Institut Montaigne/Fondation Jean-Jaurès, September 2024; Les Echos, September 2024; L'Opinion, September 2024.

### 6. The Bardella-Le Pen Split (Documented)

Within the Rassemblement National, a documented divergence exists between Marine Le Pen's 2022 platform and Jordan Bardella's 2024–2026 restatements.

| Issue | Le Pen (2022) | Bardella (2024–2026) |
|-------|---------------|----------------------|
| Retirement age | 60, unconditionally | 60 only for long careers; conditional on "budget audit" |
| Economic orientation | Described by her inner circle as "social sovereignism" | Described by his inner circle as "liberal sovereignism"; reportedly viewed by Le Pen allies as more market-oriented (Challenges, 2024) |
| Relationship with employers' organisations (patronat) | Suspicious | Active outreach: Medef lunch (April 2026), Ethic network address, "pacte de confiance" letter |

This divergence is relevant because it shows that the RN's economic platform is not fixed. It has shifted from a more statist orientation under Le Pen to a more market-friendly pitch under Bardella — while retaining the same headline promises (VAT cuts, tax relief, budget clawback). The contradiction between a market-facing pitch to business and a populist pitch to voters creates the perpetual revision cycle documented by Les Echos.

**Sources:** Challenges, 2024; Le JDD, September 2025; Les Echos, April 2026.

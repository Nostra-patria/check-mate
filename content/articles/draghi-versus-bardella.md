**Scope and methodology.** This document compares two publicly documented positions on European economic integration: the 2024 Draghi report and the 2024–2026 economic platform of Jordan Bardella and the Rassemblement National. For a chronological record of the Rassemblement National's foreign lending history, see also: *The FCRB Transaction* (Check Mate reference document, 2026). All claims about either position are drawn from the original documents, official statements, and independent institutional assessments. This record does not assert that one position is superior; it identifies where the two positions point in opposite directions and records externally produced evaluations of fiscal feasibility. Normative judgments are left to the reader.

---

### 1. Executive Summary

In July 2024, Mario Draghi delivered to the European Commission a report titled *The Future of European Competitiveness*. It concluded that the EU faces an annual investment shortfall of EUR 750–800 billion and recommended a "quantum leap" in integration: joint borrowing, completion of the single market, a European capital markets union, and expanded qualified majority voting (QMV) in foreign policy and taxation.

In June 2024 and again in June 2026, Jordan Bardella outlined a platform that represents the structural opposite: cutting France's net EU budget contribution by approximately 50%, reinstating internal Schengen border controls, blocking Ukraine's accession, and retaining the French nuclear deterrent as a strictly national asset.

**Key points:**

- The Draghi report (July 2024) identifies an annual investment gap of EUR 750–800 billion and recommends deeper EU integration, including joint borrowing and expanded QMV.
- The Bardella platform (2024–2026) proposes the structural opposite: reduced EU budget contributions, national border controls, and preservation of the national veto.
- Independent economic institutions (Asteres, OFCE, Institut Montaigne) project a potential deficit of 6.4% of GDP under the RN program; the RN disputes these assumptions.
- Both positions face political opposition: Draghi's from member states wary of further integration; Bardella's from EU institutional and fiscal frameworks.

### 2. The Draghi Report (2024)

#### 2.1 Mandate and Scope

The European Commission appointed Mario Draghi — former Prime Minister of Italy (2021–2022) and President of the European Central Bank (2011–2019) — in September 2023 to assess European competitiveness. The mandate covered innovation, decarbonisation and energy security, and defence. The final report was published in July 2024.

#### 2.2 Key Findings

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Annual investment gap | EUR 750–800 bn | p. 12 |
| EU vs. US GDP per capita | 30% below | p. 8 |
| Defence shortfall | EUR 150–200 bn | p. 150 approx. |
| EU vs. US industrial energy cost | 2–3× higher | various |

#### 2.3 Core Recommendations

The report's central argument is that incremental reform is insufficient. Draghi writes: "We need a quantum leap, not incremental reform." The recommendations include:

1. **Joint borrowing instruments** for strategic investment (modelled on NextGenerationEU)
2. **Completion of the single market** in digital services, energy, and defence procurement
3. **European capital markets union** to reduce reliance on fragmented national banking systems
4. **Streamlined decision-making** via expanded QMV in foreign policy, taxation, and enlargement
5. **Accelerated enlargement** for Ukraine and the Western Balkans, with transitional arrangements

#### 2.4 Federal Implications and Political Reception

Draghi does not use the term "federal Europe" in the report. However, each major recommendation — joint debt instruments, EU-level defence procurement, a harmonised corporate tax base, and QMV in taxation — presupposes institutional structures that exceed the current level of EU integration.

The report attracted political opposition at the European Council of December 2024, where Hungary's Viktor Orbán threatened to veto Ukraine accession talks unless QMV expansion was dropped. The Netherlands, Sweden, and Denmark have also expressed reservations about joint borrowing and QMV in taxation. Some economists have questioned whether deeper centralisation is the appropriate response, noting that the EU's most competitive regions are not necessarily the most integrated, and that the GDP gap with the United States also reflects structural factors — demographics, energy endowment, venture-capital depth — that institutional integration alone cannot resolve.

**Sources:** European Commission, "The Future of European Competitiveness" (Draghi report), July 2024; European Council conclusions, December 2024; Politico Europe, December 2024.

### 3. The Bardella Platform

#### 3.1 Origins and Documentation

The Rassemblement National has published economic policy documents since September 2024. The primary document is a "livret économique" (economic booklet), first released in September 2024 and subsequently revised. Additional statements have been made through speeches to the employers' federation Medef (April 2026), the Ethic network, and a "pacte de confiance" letter to business leaders (September 2025) whose content has not been publicly disclosed beyond slogans.

Multiple independent economic institutions have evaluated the RN program. Their assessments characterise it as internally inconsistent and fiscally expansionary, though the RN disputes these characterisations.

#### 3.2 Budget and Trade Positions

| Position | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| EU budget contribution | Cut France's net contribution by ~50% (EUR 2–3 bn annually) |
| Schengen | Reinstate national border controls |
| Free trade | Moratorium on new EU free trade agreements |
| Ukraine accession | Block Ukraine's accession to the EU |
| "Plan Manger Français" | 80% French products required in school canteens |

#### 3.3 Tax and Spending Promises

The RN has not published a fully costed fiscal roadmap. Independent reviewers have estimated the following costs based on published proposals:

- **VAT reduction on energy (20% → 5.5%):** EUR 11–17 billion annually (OFCE, Institut Montaigne, 2024)
- **Tax cuts for under-30s, doctors, nurses, and essential goods:** Revenue loss not separately modelled by the RN; independent analysts characterise it as significant
- **Employer charge relief (up to 3× minimum wage):** Significant, per Asteres, 2024
- **Retirement age rollback to 60 (conditional):** Substantial pension liability increase; conditional on "budget audit" per Bardella (2024–2026)
- **Proposed offsets:** "Fraud fighting," EU budget clawback, and abolition of unspecified agencies (RN documents; not independently modelled)

#### 3.4 Independent Assessments of Fiscal Feasibility

| Institution | Assessment |
|-------------|-----------|
| Asteres, 2024 | Public deficit to 6.4% of GDP; public debt to exceed 117% within the term |
| Allianz Research, 2024 | Revenue proposals cover "only a fraction" of promised spending |
| Institut Montaigne + Jean-Jaurès, Sept. 2024 | Program described as "costly and inconsistent" |
| OFCE, June 2024 | Independent evaluation: program fiscally expansionary |
| Lubochinsky, Cercle des Economistes, 2024 | RN funding ambitions "unrealistic" |

The European Commission's Stability and Growth Pact requires deficits below 3% of GDP. A deficit of 6.4% would place France in breach of these criteria.

**The RN's response.** The party disputes these projections on four grounds: administrative savings and fraud-combat measures would offset a portion of costs; reducing the net EU contribution and abolishing unspecified agencies would free resources; supply-side effects from VAT cuts would partially compensate for revenue loss; and France's public debt already stood at ~111% of GDP and its deficit above 5% under the Macron/Barnier government. These rebuttals have not been independently modelled and are recorded here as the party's stated position.

#### 3.5 Potential Tensions (Externally Identified)

1. **Budget contribution vs. single-market benefit:** France is a net beneficiary of the EU single market via CAP, cohesion funds, Airbus supply-chain integration, and nuclear research programmes. The EUR 2–3 billion "saving" would, according to independent trade economists, be offset by losses in market access and programme receipts.

2. **"Plan Manger Français" vs. export dependence:** France is the EU's largest agricultural and industrial exporter. An 80% domestic sourcing requirement in public procurement would conflict with France's broader interest in open EU markets for its exporters.

3. **Energy VAT cut vs. EU law:** A reduction from 20% to 5.5% would fall below the EU directive minimum floor of 15% for energy products, exposing France to legal challenge before the Court of Justice.

4. **Moratorium on free trade vs. CAP dependency:** A moratorium on new agreements would not reverse existing access but signals a departure from the market-opening posture French agriculture has historically relied upon.

5. **Exit from EU electricity market:** Independent energy analysts have warned that this risks supply shortages without cross-border interconnection backup, particularly given France's uncertain nuclear fleet renewal timeline.

**Sources:** EurActiv, June 2024; Les Echos, September 2024; L'Opinion, September 2024; Challenges, 2024; La Croix, September 2024; Asteres, 2024; OFCE, June 2024; Institut Montaigne/Fondation Jean-Jaurès, September 2024.

### 4. Five Fault Lines

**1. Budget and investment**
- *Draghi:* Joint EU borrowing; close EUR 750–800 bn annual gap collectively
- *Bardella:* Cut France's net EU contribution by 50%; oppose joint borrowing

**2. Defence**
- *Draghi:* EU-level joint procurement and shared R&D; close EUR 150–200 bn annual shortfall
- *Bardella:* National procurement only; French deterrent strictly national

**3. Single market and borders**
- *Draghi:* Complete digital, energy, and capital markets union; remove internal barriers
- *Bardella:* Reinstate Schengen border controls; exit EU electricity market mechanism

**4. Enlargement**
- *Draghi:* Accelerate Ukraine and Western Balkans accession before 2027
- *Bardella:* Block Ukraine accession indefinitely; slow Balkans process

**5. Decision-making**
- *Draghi:* Expand QMV to foreign policy, taxation, and enlargement
- *Bardella:* Preserve national veto in all areas

Each fault line marks a policy area where the Draghi and Bardella positions prescribe opposite institutional directions. The Draghi report argues that fragmentation impedes European competitiveness relative to the United States and China. The Bardella platform proposes greater national autonomy in budget, borders, and decision-making. Whether fragmentation or autonomy is preferable is a normative question; this document records only that the two platforms point in opposite directions.

**Broader context.** Draghi's recommendations are not universally accepted within the EU: the Netherlands, Sweden, and Denmark have resisted joint borrowing and QMV expansion. Similarly, France's public debt already stood at ~111% of GDP in 2024 — a level predating the RN platform that reflects structural fiscal challenges shared by several eurozone member states.

### 5. The Bardella-Le Pen Split (Documented)

Within the Rassemblement National, a documented divergence exists between Marine Le Pen's 2022 platform and Jordan Bardella's 2024–2026 restatements:

**Retirement age**
- *Le Pen (2022):* 60, unconditionally
- *Bardella (2024–2026):* 60 only for long careers; conditional on "budget audit"

**Economic orientation**
- *Le Pen (2022):* "Social sovereignism"
- *Bardella (2024–2026):* "Liberal sovereignism" — reportedly viewed by Le Pen allies as more market-oriented *(Challenges, 2024)*

**Relations with employers**
- *Le Pen (2022):* Suspicious
- *Bardella (2024–2026):* Active outreach — Medef lunch (April 2026), Ethic network, "pacte de confiance" letter

This divergence shows that the RN's economic platform is not fixed. It has shifted from a more statist orientation under Le Pen to a more market-friendly pitch under Bardella — while retaining the same headline promises (VAT cuts, tax relief, budget clawback). The contradiction between a market-facing pitch to business and a populist pitch to voters creates the perpetual revision cycle documented by Les Echos.

**Sources:** Challenges, 2024; Le JDD, September 2025; Les Echos, April 2026.

# The Kremlin Loan

## How Russia Bankrolled Marine Le Pen — and How Jordan Bardella Buried the Receipts

**Marine Le Pen walked into the Kremlin on 24 March 2017 with a smile that made headlines.** Vladimir Putin had invited her to Moscow one month before the first round of the French presidential election. The photographs were diplomatic gold for a candidate selling herself as the anti-system outsider: a French nationalist shaking hands with the Russian strongman, both of them framed by gilded curtains and the implicit promise that the old Western order was cracking.

What the cameras did not capture was the balance sheet.

Three years earlier, Le Pen's party — then still called the Front National — had taken out a loan of **€9.4 million** from the **First Czech-Russian Bank** in Moscow. The money was meant to finance the very presidential campaign Le Pen was now running. The bank's ownership led back to the orbit of **Gennady Timchenko**, a billionaire oil trader and close associate of Putin who had been placed under US sanctions in 2014. The loan was not a secret, but it was treated as a footnote. In Moscow, it was anything but.

What Russia had bought was not influence in the abstract. It was leverage over a candidate who, by the time she sat down with Putin, owed Moscow millions.

---

## The Loan

In 2014, French banks refused to lend to the Front National. The party's creditworthiness was poor and its reputation toxic. So Le Pen turned east.

The **First Czech-Russian Bank** was not a household name. It was a mid-sized Moscow lender with ties to Russian state interests and a balance sheet that would collapse within two years. In 2016, Russia's central bank revoked its licence. The RN's debt did not disappear; it was sold, first to a Moscow car-rental firm called **Konti**, and later to **Aviazapchast**, a Russian aerospace and spare-parts company.

That transfer should have set off alarms. Aviazapchast was not a normal creditor.

A 2018 investigation by the **German Marshall Fund** found that Aviazapchast was involved in exports to the **Syrian Ministry of Defence** and maintained what the report called "high-priority strategic partnerships for the Russian state." The firm's leadership included personnel with links to Russian military and intelligence structures. A French presidential campaign was now indebted to a company that sat at the intersection of Russian defence procurement and state security.

This was no longer a party-finance problem. It was a national-security problem wearing a loan agreement.

---

## The Dinner

Le Pen's 2017 Moscow visit was officially about foreign policy. She used the platform to call for an end to EU sanctions against Russia and to praise Putin as a defender of national sovereignty.

But the optics were impossible to ignore. Russian state media — **Life News**, **Interfax** — ran stories suggesting the Kremlin was actively backing her campaign. Interfax briefly published a dispatch stating that the Kremlin had announced financing for Le Pen's election bid by Russian banks, then retracted it. The episode had the unmistakable air of a trial balloon: testing how much the French public would tolerate, while keeping just enough deniability.

Le Pen returned to France with photographs and talking points. She also returned with the debt still on her party's books, now held by a military-linked Russian firm, accruing interest in a Moscow ledger.

---

## The Lawsuit

By December 2019, Aviazapchast had had enough. The creditor filed suit against the RN in a **Moscow court**, demanding repayment of what had grown to roughly **€11 million** with interest. The lawsuit was a public humiliation: a French political party, claiming to fight for French sovereignty, being dragged before a Russian judge by a defence-sector firm for failure to pay its Moscow debts.

The RN settled in mid-2020. The terms were not fully disclosed, but the principle was established: the debt was real, enforceable, and Russian.

---

## The Repayment — and the Reveal

The loan was finally cleared in **September 2023**.

RN treasurer **Kévin Pfeffer** transferred **€6,088,784** to close the account. The payment was made with **public funding** — French state party financing, taxpayer money — to settle a debt that had originated in a Moscow bank tied to Putin's inner circle.

The timing was not accidental. Jordan Bardella, by then the party's 28-year-old president, had **accelerated the repayment** explicitly to "close the chapter" before the **2024 European Parliament elections**. The RN was rebranding itself as a mainstream, electable conservative force. A Russian loan on the books was an inconvenient reminder of where the party had actually come from.

So French citizens paid, through state allocations, to scrub a Kremlin debt from a party that claims to put France first.

---

## The "Naïvité" Defense

Bardella has since tried to draw a line between himself and the party's old pro-Putin reflexes. In 2023, he called the RN's historical stance toward Russia a case of **"collective naïvité."** He now describes Russia as a **"multi-dimensional threat"** to Europe and has positioned himself as more Atlanticist than Le Pen.

But the voting record tells a different story.

RN members in the European Parliament have **abstained from or opposed** resolutions condemning Russian aggression. According to **Nicolas Tenzer**, a foreign-policy scholar at Sciences Po, the RN has **never voted in favour** of a single pro-Ukraine or anti-Russia resolution in either the French or European Parliament. Bardella himself opposes sending French troops to Ukraine and has resisted the provision of long-range missiles to Kyiv.

The old guard, meanwhile, has not changed. **Thierry Mariani**, the RN's veteran MEP, still defends Russia's occupation of Crimea and parrots the Kremlin narrative that NATO expansion triggered the war. A French defence official described the party to Politico as split into three camps: Mariani's traditional pro-Russians, a small Atlanticist faction around Bardella, and a large middle group that is "fundamentally pro-Russian" but knows better than to say so before an election.

Former French ambassador **Michel Foucher** put it more bluntly. The RN's rhetoric, he warned, is marked by **"doublespeak"** — and if the party ever took power, the Kremlin would find ready-made **"agents of influence and information."**

---

## What the Loan Bought

The Kremlin did not need to write policy for the RN. It only needed to make sure the party remembered who had extended credit when no one else would.

For nine years — from 2014 to 2023 — a French presidential candidate and her party carried a debt to a Russian military-linked firm. They met with Putin while the debt was active. They settled a lawsuit in Moscow. They repaid the balance with public money, quietly, once the electoral calendar demanded a cleaner image.

The loan was not just financing. It was a marker. A reminder that when French banks said no, Moscow said yes — and that the debt, long after it was signed, kept the National Rally on a leash that stretched from Paris to Moscow.

Bardella wants France to believe that chapter is closed. The receipts say otherwise.

# Tommy Robinson: The Convict, the Kremlin, and the Musk Family

## The Man in the Moscow Hotel

On 9 June 2026, a man who has served five prison sentences walked into the Shalyapin Grand Lobby and Bar of Moscow's five-star Metropol Hotel and sat down with Errol Musk — the 80-year-old father of the world's richest man. He pulled out his phone, hit record, and told the world: "We are going to go and cause some trouble." Errol Musk, sitting opposite, replied calmly: "No trouble, we are doing things right."

Within 48 hours, that same man had posted a list of 70 protest sites across the United Kingdom, called migrants "invaders" and "savages," and told his two million followers: "It's time." When violence broke out in Belfast over a knife attack by a Sudanese national, he added the clarifying footnote: "Whilst I absolutely DO NOT advocate for violence or for setting a town ablaze, I can understand the anger that drives it."

To the press, he explained why he was in Moscow. "Russia is not the enemy of Britain," he said. "That narrative has long since died a natural death. There are those who benefit from pushing Russia as an enemy but everyone laughs at those people now." He said he had come "to see how this country got itself so well on to the straight and narrow and see the beauty of a civilised society here."

This man does not exist in a vacuum. He is Stephen Christopher Yaxley-Lennon, better known as Tommy Robinson — founder of the English Defence League, convicted fraudster, serial jailbird, and, as it turns out, someone who has been on the Russian payroll for longer than most people realise.

## Who He Actually Is

Before the stage name, the documentaries, and the X premium subscriptions, there was Stephen Yaxley: born 27 November 1982 in Luton, the son of an Irish mother who worked at a bakery and a Vauxhall plant. He apprenticed as an aircraft engineer, got the job 600 people applied for, and lost it in 2005 when he was convicted of assaulting an off-duty police officer in a drunken brawl. He served 12 months.

The name "Tommy Robinson" came from a Luton Town football hooligan. The pseudonym worked well enough to hide his record until 2010, when *Searchlight* magazine connected the dots. By then he had already co-founded the English Defence League in 2009 — an organisation the BBC described as "loosely affiliated with football hooligan firms" and whose members described themselves, simply, as anti-Islam.

Robinson's rap sheet since then reads like a career criminal's curriculum vitae: common assault, threats, harassment, mortgage fraud, contempt of court (multiple times), breach of bail, use of a false passport. He has served five prison terms between 2005 and 2025. In 2021 he declared bankruptcy, admitting to £100,000 in gambling losses. By August 2024, *The Times* reported he owed roughly £2 million to creditors and was under HMRC investigation for unpaid taxes.

This is the man who now advises on British immigration policy from a Moscow hotel bar.

## The Goodwill Ambassador — 2017

Here is the fact that reframes everything. Years before Tommy Robinson ever set foot in Russia, he was already on a Russian payroll.

In 2017, an unregistered charity called the MMBF Trust — the "Matthew Martino Benevolent Fund" — announced that Robinson had been appointed its "goodwill ambassador." The trust claimed to support filmmakers and performing arts students. It was not registered with the UK's Charity Commission. It did not, as far as anyone can tell, support any filmmakers.

What it did support was the *London Post* — a website that looks, on the surface, like a local news outlet covering London restaurant reviews and gambling regulations. Behind that facade, according to an investigation by *Byline Times* published in February 2026, the *London Post* is part of a "black PR" operation: coordinated disinformation campaigns designed to manipulate public opinion while concealing their true sponsors.

The network works like this. The *London Post* belongs to 2Trom News Group, incorporated in the UK but reportedly first set up in Gibraltar. Its owners: a Russian named Viktor Tokarev, and a British executive named Lewis Drazen. The visible face is Matthew C Martino, a Zimbabwean-English film producer who hired Robinson as ambassador.

But the money and direction come from Moscow Media Group (MMG), a Russian communications conglomerate owned by Igor Kuzin. MMG's subsidiary, MMG Brainstorming, advertises its work openly: "information campaigns," "reputation management," "government relations communications." Its client list includes the Presidential Administration of the Russian Federation, the State Duma, the Federation Council, and various ministries.

MMG also has commercial ties to Gazprom Neft — the oil subsidiary of Gazprom, subject to US, UK, and EU sanctions. Kuzin's IT consultant, Vasily Maveev, simultaneously works for Smart Analytics Russia, whose clients include Rosstat, the Russian Post, and the Analytical Centre for the Government of the Russian Federation.

Alexander Chernavsky, Kuzin's partner at MMG Brainstorming, runs the Military Orthodox Mission and the Mission for the Revival of Traditional Values of the Russian People. In 2022 he presented the Order of Nicholas II to Maria Zakharova — Russia's foreign ministry spokesperson, sanctioned by both the EU and US as a "central figure of government propaganda" who "promoted the deployment of Russian forces in Ukraine."

Tommy Robinson, then, was not some naive tourist who wandered into Moscow and got impressed by the architecture. In 2017, he accepted a title from a propaganda operation with direct commercial ties to Vladimir Putin's administration, Russia's sanctioned oil industry, and the foreign ministry's chief disinformation officer. He was, in effect, an ambassador for the Kremlin before he ever needed a Russian visa.

## The 2020 Reconnaissance

When Robinson did visit Russia in early 2020, he produced a behind-the-scenes video of his trip to Moscow and St. Petersburg. Standing in a skyscraper, he gushed about the city: "It's clean. It's beautiful. I go to London and I don't like London. I hate London." He joked about moving to Russia. He wanted his wife to see it.

Throughout the video, his patron was referred to only as "the host." He never said who paid.

The *New York Times* reported in April 2021 that the trip was "actually a front for his real purpose, which was to seek out Russian bank accounts where he could hide his money." His chaperone, Edvard Chesnokov, confirmed to the newspaper that Robinson had discussed opening bank accounts. The idea, reportedly, came from Paul Golding, leader of Britain First — another far-right figure with his own history of pro-Putin pilgrimages. Robinson denied it. Golding said there had been "no mention" of bank accounts. Chesnokov said there had been.

During the same trip, Robinson gave an interview to Russia Today defending Bashar al-Assad, claiming reports of chemical weapons use were "total propaganda" designed to get British public support for war. He told *Vechernyaya Moskva* that Russia had nothing to do with the 2018 Skripal poisonings in Salisbury: "If Russia needed to remove him, they would do it quietly, without unnecessary noise, without the use of Russian chemical weapons. It was stupid." He said he would like to have a beer with Putin and urged him to give him a Russian television show.

When mainstream platforms banned him, he joined VK — a Russian social media service. When Ukraine was invaded in 2022, he amplified Sputnik News claims that Ukraine was bombing its own civilians and blaming Russia. According to anti-fascist researchers at HOPE not hate, he became, functionally, "Putin's useful idiot."

## The Amplifier

In the summer of 2024, riots erupted across Britain after a mass stabbing in Southport. Robinson was in Cyprus at the time, but his disinformation helped fuel them. In August 2024, former MI6 officer Christopher Steele — the man behind the Trump-Russia dossier — publicly stated that British security services would investigate who was funding Robinson. Steele noted that far-right actors had amplified false claims during the riots that had been manufactured by Russia-linked sites.

By then, Robinson had a new and powerful amplifier: Elon Musk.

In January 2025, *Politico* confirmed that Musk had agreed to pay the legal fees for two specific cases on Robinson's behalf. During the UK's racist riots the previous summer, Musk had amplified Robinson's posts on X. In September 2025, Musk called for a change of the British government at Robinson's London rally. When Nigel Farage objected, Musk cut ties with Farage and doubled down on Robinson. By June 2026, *The i Paper* reported that Musk was a paid subscriber to Robinson's premium X posts and was actively sharing Robinson's content calling for protests — as well as posts from Rupert Lowe's "Restore Britain" party, a far-right challenger to Reform UK.

The billionaire's father, meanwhile, was in St. Petersburg last week at the Kremlin-backed International Economic Forum — Russia's answer to Davos — where he praised Putin as a "very stable and pleasant man" and complained that Western "fake media" pushes "complete nonsense" about Russia.

Two days later, Errol Musk was in the Metropol Hotel with Tommy Robinson, agreeing that they were "doing things right."

## The June 2026 Convergence

Robinson was not the only Western figure in Russia that week. Andrew Tate and his brother Tristan were also posting from Moscow. Candace Owens and Alex Jones had attended the St. Petersburg forum. The Kremlin has made a deliberate project of courting Western far-right figures — anti-liberal voices who can be amplified, funded, and sent home to say exactly what Robinson said: that Russia is civilised, that the West lies about it, that the real enemy is not Moscow but the migrants, the Muslims, the "globalists."

Dan Lomas, an intelligence expert at the University of Nottingham, described the pattern to *The i Paper*: "They would come back to Britain and say, 'Oh my god, you know, Russia is great'... I think this is a similar strategy." He called Robinson a "useful idiot" in the classic sense: ideologically motivated, invited to Russia, treated to expensive surroundings, then deployed.

Sophie Williams-Dunning, a researcher at RUSI — the Royal United Services Institute, Britain's oldest defence and security think tank — was more direct: "Russia is one of the most prolific actors when it comes to conducting hybrid campaigns to influence the politics of other countries, including the UK. These campaigns blend online influence, cyber-attacks and other measures to try to cause offline effects — and events such as the Belfast attack certainly have the kind of incendiary potential that Moscow may seek to harness."

She added: "If Tommy Robinson is trying to stoke unrest in the UK along these lines, it's not unreasonable to assume that his agenda complements the Kremlin's aim of sowing division, undermining public trust and exacerbating crises."

Calvin Bailey, a Labour MP on parliament's defence committee, put it in the plainest possible terms: "This is deadly serious. Political warfare is central to Russia's aggression against the UK and Europe."

## The Pattern, Not the Conspiracy

No one is claiming that Tommy Robinson receives coded instructions from the GRU. That is not how this works — and that is precisely why it works so well.

What the record shows is a man with a criminal history, a debt problem, a bankruptcy, a gambling addiction, and a desperate need for funding and attention. In 2017, he accepted a title from a Russian propaganda network linked to sanctioned oil companies and the Putin administration. In 2020, he explored moving his money to Russian banks while giving interviews denying Russian war crimes and chemical weapons attacks. In 2022, he spread Kremlin disinformation about Ukraine. In 2024, his content helped fuel racist riots while a former MI6 chief suggested monitoring him for Russian links. In 2025, the world's richest man began funding his legal defence and amplifying his message to 200 million followers. In June 2026, he sat in a Moscow hotel with that billionaire's father, filmed himself promising to "cause trouble," returned to Britain in spirit if not in body, and lit 70 fires.

He is not a spy. He is better than a spy. He is a convict with a grievance, a microphone, and a billionaire's credit line — who has been taking money and legitimacy from Russian interests for nearly a decade while telling the British public that Russia is not the enemy.

The enemy, of course, is whoever he is told to point at next.

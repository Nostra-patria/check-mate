# The Inspector Who Told the Truth, Then Lost the Plot

**How a UN weapons inspector who correctly predicted Iraq had no weapons of mass destruction became a convicted sex offender, a Kremlin mouthpiece, and a guest of honour at Putin's economic forum**

---

In the winter of 2002, as the Bush administration built its case for war against Iraq, one man kept saying what no one in Washington wanted to hear.

Scott Ritter, the chief United Nations weapons inspector who had spent seven years hunting for Saddam Hussein's chemical, biological, and nuclear programmes, told anyone who would listen that the weapons were gone. Destroyed. Disassembled. "Absent such evidence," he said at Harvard that September, "the United States is ill-advised to go to war."

He was right. The war happened anyway. Hundreds of thousands died. No weapons were found. And for a brief moment, Scott Ritter looked like one of the last honest men in American public life — a former Marine intelligence officer who had chosen truth over career, fact over flag-waving, evidence over ideology.

Eight years later, a jury in Stroudsburg, Pennsylvania, found him guilty of six felonies for masturbating on camera in front of a person he believed was a 15-year-old girl.

And fifteen years after that, he was sipping cocktails in St. Petersburg, being interviewed by Russian state media as a "brave truth-teller" silenced by Washington, while seated at the same forum that welcomed Andrew Tate and Steven Seagal.

This is not a redemption story.

---

## The Weapons That Weren't There

Ritter's credentials were, by any measure, impressive. A former US Marine Corps intelligence officer, he had served as a junior analyst during Operation Desert Storm before joining UNSCOM, the UN Special Commission charged with dismantling Iraq's weapons programmes. From 1991 to 1998, he led inspections, confronted Iraqi officials, and built a reputation for unflinching thoroughness.

He resigned in 1998, accusing the US and UN of failing to get tough with Saddam Hussein. But his subsequent evolution was unexpected: rather than becoming a hawk, he became one of the most prominent critics of the coming war. He argued, correctly and repeatedly, that Iraq had destroyed its WMDs and that there was no evidence of new programmes.

In the superheated months before the March 2003 invasion, Ritter's warnings were unwelcome. He was called unpatriotic, a Saddam apologist, a dupe. Fox News hosts attacked him. Members of Congress implied he was on Iraq's payroll. The neoconservative machinery that had been constructed to sell the war had no patience for a former inspector who kept producing inspection reports showing nothing.

When the invasion came and went, and the search teams found exactly what Ritter had predicted — nothing — his earlier ostracism looked, in retrospect, like one of the great acts of professional courage in modern American foreign policy. He had been right when it mattered. He had been right when being right cost him his reputation, his access, and his livelihood.

But being right about WMDs did not make him right about everything. And it did not make him good.

## The Chatroom

Ritter had been arrested once before, in 2001, in an online sex sting in West Albany, New York. The charges — attempting to meet a minor for sex — were dismissed on a technicality. The identity of the undercover officer had not been properly revealed to the judge. Ritter walked free.

He did not, apparently, walk away.

On February 7, 2009, Ritter entered a Yahoo chatroom and began exchanging sexually explicit messages with someone he believed to be a 15-year-old girl named "Emily." Emily was, in fact, an undercover detective in Barrett Township, Pennsylvania, conducting an online sting operation.

The chat logs, later introduced as evidence, were graphic and unambiguous. Ritter performed a sex act on himself via webcam. The "girl" repeatedly stated her age — 15. Ritter continued. When the detective asked if he was worried about getting caught, Ritter allegedly replied that he was "experienced" and knew how to avoid detection.

He was arrested in November 2009. The case dragged through the courts for more than a year — an appellate court briefly ruled some evidence inadmissible — but in April 2011, a jury in Stroudsburg convicted him on all six counts. The charges included unlawful contact with a minor, criminal use of a communications facility, corruption of minors, indecent exposure, possessing instruments of crime, and criminal solicitation.

Assistant District Attorney Michael Rakaczewski, after the verdict: "The jury reached the right decision. They saw the case for what it is and the defendant for what he is and what he did."

Ritter's defence attorney, Gary Kohlman, told the jury they would be "troubled and offended" by the graphic chat and video evidence, but insisted the case turned on Ritter's intent. Ritter himself took the stand and claimed he believed "Emily" was an adult acting out a fantasy.

The jury did not believe him. He was sentenced to between one and a half and four years in state prison. He was paroled in December 2014.

## The Pivot

If Ritter's pre-2011 trajectory had been from military officer to truth-teller, his post-2011 trajectory was something else entirely. The Iraq War critic who had once challenged power on factual grounds gradually transformed into a commentator who served power — Russian power — on ideological ones.

He became a regular contributor to RT, the Russian state-funded television network that the US Department of Justice has required to register as a foreign agent. He wrote for Sputnik, the Russian state news agency. He visited Russia repeatedly after the February 2022 full-scale invasion of Ukraine, each time delivering commentary that mirrored Kremlin talking points: that NATO expansion justified the invasion, that Ukraine was run by Nazis, that the West was the real aggressor.

In June 2024, Ritter claimed that US authorities had seized his passport and prevented him from traveling to Russia. He framed it as political persecution — the US government punishing him for his dissenting views. The claim was repeated across Russian state media and sympathetic Western outlets.

What was rarely mentioned alongside it: that Ritter was a convicted sex offender on parole, and that convicted felons often face travel restrictions as a routine condition of supervised release. The passport seizure was almost certainly administrative, not political. But in Ritter's narrative, and in the Kremlin media ecosystem that amplified him, it became proof that Washington was silencing a truth-teller.

It was a masterful reframing. A man convicted of online sexual conduct with a minor became a victim of state censorship. A felon under supervised release became a dissident. The same rhetorical skill that had once been deployed to expose false WMD claims was now being deployed to obscure a criminal record.

## SPIEF 2026

The St. Petersburg International Economic Forum is Putin's answer to Davos — an annual gathering of oligarchs, officials, and foreign fellow-travellers designed to project Russian economic vitality despite international sanctions. In 2026, the guest of honour was Saudi Arabia. The actual guest list was a parade of the compromised.

Andrew Tate, facing 21 criminal charges including rape and human trafficking across three countries, arrived to folk dancers and bread-and-salt. Steven Seagal, the actor who accepted Russian citizenship in 2016 and has been banned from Ukraine as a national security threat, posed for photographs. And Scott Ritter, convicted sex offender and former UN inspector, delivered interviews from the forum floor about how Putin's economic vision was being "decoded" by the West.

A Latvian podcast, The Eastern Border, recorded the scene with weary accuracy: "While the Russian elite dine on 18,500-ruble elk lips at SPIEF alongside Andrew Tate and Scott Ritter, the empire is bleeding out."

Ritter was interviewed by Consortium News from the forum. A Facebook post by Aimee Kligman circulated widely: "Scott Ritter at St. Petersburg: the man Washington tried to silence." The YouTube algorithm promoted his analysis. The Kremlin media apparatus distributed it.

The irony was almost too perfect. The man who had once exposed state deception was now participating in it — not as a victim, but as a willing performer. The "truth-teller" who had been right about Iraq's WMDs was now telling Russian television audiences that Putin's war was defensive, that NATO was the aggressor, that the West was morally bankrupt. And he was doing it from a forum that platformed convicted criminals and propaganda agents while Russia's economy contracted and its army stalled in eastern Ukraine.

## The Credibility Trap

There is a particular danger in cases like Ritter's, and it is worth naming. Ritter's early work was genuinely valuable. His UNSCOM inspections were rigorous. His opposition to the Iraq War was factually grounded. His 2002 warnings were correct when most of official Washington was wrong.

This early credibility creates what might be called a "credibility trap": the assumption that because someone was right once, they are right always. That because a person challenged one state's lies, they challenge all states' lies. That expertise in one domain transfers to expertise in all domains, and that moral courage in one context immunises against moral failure in another.

None of these assumptions hold. Ritter was right about Iraqi WMDs because he had inspected Iraqi weapons sites, not because he possessed a generalised truth-telling superpower. His expertise in arms control gave him no special insight into Ukrainian sovereignty, NATO strategy, or the legality of Russia's invasion. And his willingness to challenge the Bush administration in 2002 said nothing about his willingness to shill for the Putin administration in 2026.

What happened to Ritter between 2002 and 2026 is not fully knowable from the outside. The 2011 conviction suggests a personality that did not merely dissent from American foreign policy but had come to resent American institutions more broadly — including, apparently, the legal norms governing online conduct with minors. The post-conviction pivot to Russian media suggests either genuine ideological alignment or a transactional relationship: a platform in exchange for a narrative.

Perhaps it was both. Perhaps Ritter, embittered by his treatment by American media and courts, found in Russian state television a welcoming audience that asked no inconvenient questions about his criminal record. Perhaps the same contrarian impulse that had led him to challenge consensus on Iraq led him to challenge consensus on Ukraine — but this time without the factual foundation that had grounded his earlier dissent. Perhaps the loneliness of being a pariah in one country made the embrace of another irresistible, even if that other country was actively committing war crimes.

Whatever the psychology, the result is the same. The man who once exposed one government's deception now amplifies another's. The inspector who searched for hidden weapons now performs for a propaganda network that hides casualties. The truth-teller has become a useful lie.

## The Forum and the Felons

SPIEF 2026 was not an aberration. It was a culmination — a gathering that revealed what kind of "international" forum Putin's Russia actually hosts. Not a forum of serious economists and policymakers, but a forum of the compromised, the convicted, and the credulous.

Andrew Tate, accused human trafficker, received a folk-dance welcome. Steven Seagal, propagandist and Putin loyalist, posed for cameras. Scott Ritter, convicted sex offender, delivered geopolitical analysis. Each brought something the Kremlin needed: Tate brought youth-oriented grievance content; Seagal brought celebrity legitimacy; Ritter brought the particular authority of a former US official who could claim to have "seen through" American hypocrisy.

The pattern is by now familiar from other Kremlin-backed events. The platform is not offered despite the criminal records — it is offered, in part, because of them. A man with 21 pending charges has limited options for international travel and public legitimacy. A convicted sex offender has limited options for respectable platforms. These constraints make the Kremlin's embrace more valuable, more binding, more absolute. The forum becomes not merely a stage but a shelter. The propaganda becomes not merely a message but a lifeline.

Ritter's presence at SPIEF was, in this light, not a contradiction of his earlier work but a continuation of its late-stage logic. The man who had once challenged state deception from the left had become a man who served state deception from the right. The inspector who searched for truth had become a performer who sold narrative. The only constant was the contrarian posture — but contrarianism without factual grounding is not dissent. It is merely opposition for opposition's sake, indistinguishable from propaganda.

---

## What It Means

Scott Ritter is not the most important figure in the Kremlin propaganda ecosystem. He is not the most prolific, the most watched, or the most influential. But he may be the most instructive — because his case demonstrates how easily the rhetoric of anti-establishment truth-telling can be repurposed into establishment-serving deception, provided the establishment in question is foreign.

The through-line from UNSCOM inspector to RT contributor runs through a genuine grievance: Ritter was, in fact, mistreated by American media and government in 2002-2003. He was called names he did not deserve. He was sidelined for being correct. The Iraq War was, in fact, launched on false pretences, and Ritter was, in fact, one of the few who said so publicly.

But a genuine grievance does not justify every subsequent choice. Being right about WMDs does not license being wrong about Ukraine. Being mistreated by the Bush administration does not license shilling for the Putin administration. And being convicted of sex crimes involving minors does not license reframing that conviction as political persecution.

Ritter's tragedy is not that he was wrong once. It is that he was right once, and then so comprehensively wrong afterwards — about Russia, about Ukraine, about his own legal record, about the meaning of dissent — that his early correctness now functions as a shield for his later dishonesty. When he appears on Russian state television and says "I was right about Iraq," the audience is supposed to conclude that he is therefore right about everything else. The syllogism is fallacious, but it is effective.

The Kremlin understands this. That is why Ritter is platformed. That is why Tate is platformed. That is why Seagal is platformed. Not despite their compromised status, but because of it — because a man with limited options is a man with limited leverage, and a man with limited leverage is a man who will say what he is asked to say.

The inspector who once searched for hidden weapons has become, himself, a weapon — wielded by one state against another, deployed in chatrooms and forums and television studios, his former credibility now ammunition in an information war he pretends to oppose.

He was right, once, when it mattered. But being right once is not the same as being good. And it is certainly not the same as being honest now.

# The Conversion: How JD Vance Went From "America's Hitler" to Trump's VP

**The man who called Donald Trump a "cynical asshole" and "America's Hitler" in 2016 now stands one heartbeat from the presidency. The pipeline that got him there — and the price he paid — is one of the most documented political transformations in modern American history.**

---

In 2016, JD Vance was exactly what American media wanted: a conservative working-class voice who could attack Donald Trump from the inside. Yale Law School graduate. Marine Corps veteran. Author of *Hillbilly Elegy*, then the most discussed book in Washington. A man who had grown up in Ohio's Rust Belt and made it to the country's finest law school.

And he hated Donald Trump.

Privately, in a Facebook group chat with Yale Law roommates, Vance wrote that he went "back and forth between thinking Trump is a cynical asshole like Nixon who wouldn't be that bad (and might even prove useful) or that he's America's Hitler." The message was not a stray remark. It was the considered verdict of a man who had spent months watching Trump up close.

Publicly, Vance was more measured but no less critical. On Facebook, he wrote that Trump made people he cared about afraid — immigrants, Muslims, people of colour. In a Medium post, he described Trump as a demagogue who would betray the working class the moment he had power.

Vance was, in 2016, a Never-Trumper with credentials. The media loved him for it.

---

## The Brand and the Reality

*Hillbilly Elegy* sold the image of an authentic working-class voice — a grandson of Kentucky hillbillies who understood the despair of closed factories and opioid addiction. The book's power came from its supposed authenticity: this wasn't a coastal journalist visiting Ohio for a weekend. This was someone who had lived it.

The reality was more complicated.

Vance was born in Middletown, Ohio, not Appalachia. After Yale Law, he did not return to the Rust Belt to rebuild communities. He went to Silicon Valley. Peter Thiel — PayPal co-founder, first external investor in Facebook, billionaire tech-libertarian — placed Vance at Mithril Capital, his venture capital fund. Vance learned the VC game, then founded his own fund, Narya Capital. He networked in Palo Alto while writing about steel mills.

The brand was working-class. The career was venture capital. Both were true, in a way. But only one was marketed.

---

## The Pipeline

The transformation from "America's Hitler" to Trump's running mate did not happen organically. It had a financier.

Peter Thiel had been watching Vance long before 2016. After Yale Law, Thiel made Vance part of his investment ecosystem. The relationship deepened over years. In 2021, Thiel organised a fundraiser. The goal was not merely money — it was to introduce JD Vance to Donald Trump.

The man who had called Trump "America's Hitler" was now being presented to him as a potential ally.

The meeting worked. Trump, who normally never forgets a personal insult, seemed to overlook Vance's earlier criticism — or perhaps valued it as evidence of Vance's intelligence. Thiel then pumped more than $10 million (reports cite up to $15 million) into the Protect Ohio Values PAC to finance Vance's 2022 Senate campaign. Vance won.

The pipeline was complete: Thiel's money → Thiel's network → Trump's endorsement → Senate seat.

---

## The Conversion

Between 2021 and 2024, Vance's public positions underwent a total inversion.

The man who once wrote that Trump made immigrants afraid became an aggressive voice for hardline immigration policy. The man who called Trump a demagogue who would betray the working class became a defender of Trump's tax cuts and deregulation. And the man who once saw Trump as a threat to democracy became an election denier.

In public statements, Vance refused to acknowledge that Trump lost the 2020 election. He described social media censorship as "a bigger threat to democracy" than the January 6th insurrection. He attacked transgender rights and migration with a vehemence his 2016 self would not recognise.

By 2024, Trump selected Vance as his running mate. The "America's Hitler" message was still on the record. Vance had never retracted it — he simply acted as if it had never existed.

---

## What He Sold

Vance did not merely change his mind. He sold a product.

The product was his reputation as an independent thinker. The man who had once been media's favourite working-class Trump critic was now rebranded as Trump's working-class defender. The credibility he had built by criticising Trump was repurposed to legitimise Trump.

This is the specific mechanics of Vance's conversion: his 2016 anti-Trump credentials gave him a credibility that pure loyalists lacked. When Vance defended Trump's election lies, it carried more weight than the same lie from a lifelong sycophant. A convert is a better witness than a born believer.

He also sold the working-class voice he had built. *Hillbilly Elegy* had made Vance the interpreter of the white working class for elite audiences. That voice was now used to sell policies — tax cuts for billionaires, deportation for immigrants — that benefited the elite while claiming to speak for the working class.

The irony is documented in Vance's own words. The man who once called Trump "reprehensible" for frightening immigrants became the voice that frightened immigrants. The man who once warned that Trump would betray the working class became the working-class mask for Trump's billionaire agenda.

---

## The Price

The price of Vance's conversion is visible in what he now defends versus what he once believed.

He stands behind a man he once called "America's Hitler." He promotes policies he once called "reprehensible." He represents a movement he once described as a threat to the people he cared about.

The voters who trusted him — the Ohio workers who read his book, the conservatives who valued his independence — received something different from what they thought they were buying. They bought authenticity. They got adaptability. They bought principles. They got a product.

---

## What It Means

Vance's story is not unique. American politics regularly produces figures who build an "authentic" brand — working-class, immigrant, outsider — and then sell that brand to the highest bidder.

What makes Vance instructive is that his transformation is so thoroughly documented. We have the group chat. We have the Facebook posts. We have the Medium essay. We have the Thiel pipeline and the $10-15 million PAC. We have the election denial on camera.

And we have the endpoint: a man who once called Donald Trump "America's Hitler" now stands one heartbeat from his presidency.

It is not a story about changing one's mind. It is a story about changing one's market. In 2016, the market wanted anti-Trump authenticity. In 2024, the market wanted pro-Trump loyalty. Vance supplied what the market demanded. His principles were not flexible — they were fictitious.

In a political system where brands matter more than biographies, where stories matter more than facts, where authenticity is a product that can be packaged and sold — there will always be JD Vances. Men who speak the right words to the right people until the right price is paid. Men who once told the truth until the truth became more expensive than the lie.

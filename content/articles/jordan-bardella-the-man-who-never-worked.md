# Jordan Bardella: The Man Who Never Worked

## The 30-year-old who wants to run France has never held a job outside politics, never finished university, and grew up in a private Catholic school while selling a banlieue origin story

On 13 September 2025, Jordan Bardella turned thirty. He had already been president of France's most powerful far-right party for three years, a Member of the European Parliament for six, and a regional councillor for a decade. What he had not been, at any point in those thirty years, was employed.

Not in a shop. Not in an office. Not in a factory, a café, a construction site, or even a summer internship at his father's firm. Bardella's only recorded pre-parliamentary employment lasted five months, from February to June 2015, when he worked as a parliamentary assistant to National Front MEP Jean-François Jalkh — a role that was, by definition, political. He was nineteen. He has never worked since.

This is not a biography quirk. It is the central fact of the man who may become the next President of France.

---

## The Origin Story

Bardella's political brand rests on a single narrative: he is the kid from the banlieue who made good. He was born on 13 September 1995 in Drancy, Seine-Saint-Denis, one of France's poorest departments, and grew up, in his own words, "on the eighth floor of a drab high-rise tower block." He has spoken publicly about how his mother "had difficulty making ends meet" and how he was "confronted with violence at an early age." The message is clear: I know these streets. I lived them.

The details, however, complicate the picture. Bardella's mother, Luisa Bertelli-Motta, was indeed a kindergarten assistant raising him alone after separating from his father. But his father, Olivier Bardella, was not absent in the way the narrative suggests. He was a small business owner — specialising in beverage vending machines — and lived in Montmorency, a wealthy suburb in Val-d'Oise. Bardella spent weekends and Wednesdays there. Critics have noted that while Bardella emphasises the social difficulties of Seine-Saint-Denis, his father had sufficient means to live in one of the department's most affluent communes.

More significantly, Bardella did not attend the state schools of Drancy. He went to the **semi-private Catholic lycée Jean-Baptiste-de-La-Salle** in Saint-Denis, where he received his baccalauréat with distinction in economics and social sciences. Euronews reported that he "attended mostly Catholic private schools." A Le Monde investigation into his childhood found former classmates who remembered him volunteering to teach French to migrants at that same private school — an image that sits awkwardly with the anti-immigration platform he now leads.

After failing the entrance exam for Sciences Po, the prestigious Paris political school, Bardella enrolled in geography at Paris-Sorbonne University. He dropped out. According to the Associated Press, which profiled him in 2025: "He never finished university."

He was already, by then, a full-time politician.

---

## The Fast Track

Bardella joined the National Front at sixteen, in 2012. He has said he joined "more for Marine Le Pen than for the National Front." Within two years, at nineteen, he became the party's youngest-ever departmental secretary, running operations in Seine-Saint-Denis. He ran for departmental office in 2015, lost, but was elected to the Regional Council of Île-de-France that same year. He was twenty.

From there, the ascent was uninterrupted. Party spokesman at twenty-two. President of the youth wing at twenty-three. Lead candidate for the European Parliament at twenty-four, becoming one of the youngest MEPs in EU history. Vice-president of the party at twenty-four. Acting president at twenty-six. Officially elected president of the National Rally — by then renamed from the Front National — at twenty-seven, with 85% of the vote.

Every rung was inside the same organisation. Every promotion came from the same patron: Marine Le Pen.

There was no gap year, no trial in the private sector, no apprenticeship, no stint as a teacher or a nurse or a shop manager or a truck driver. There was not even the classic French path of the *ena* graduate who at least passes through the civil service. Bardella went from a private Catholic school directly into party headquarters, and he has never left.

---

## The Ironies

The contradictions stack up. Bardella campaigns on immigration control and national identity, yet his own heritage is a mosaic of the very migrations his party opposes. His mother is Italian, born in Turin. His father's side is North Italian, French-Alsatian, and — a detail he has been less eager to discuss — Franco-Algerian. Moroccan media outlet Hespress reported in 2024 on his "family ties to Morocco and Algeria," noting that "while Bardella has never hidden his Italian roots, he has been less open about his family's North African ties."

He presents himself as the voice of the working class, yet he has never worked. He decries the elites, yet he attended private school and has spent his entire adult life in the insulated world of party politics, EU per diems, and Strasbourg expense accounts. He claims to represent the "forgotten territories of the Republic" — the name he gave his 2016 activist group, *Banlieues Patriotes* — yet his 2023 attendance rate at the Regional Council of Île-de-France was 27%. He missed nearly three-quarters of the sessions of the very body that governed the region he says he speaks for.

He resigned from that council in February 2025.

---

## The Inheritance

Bardella's rise has always been inseparable from Marine Le Pen's shadow. She brought him into the party, promoted him at every stage, and eventually stepped aside as party president in 2021 to focus on her own presidential ambitions. When Le Pen was convicted in 2024 of embezzling EU funds and barred from running in the 2027 presidential election, the path cleared almost by default.

Le Pen had spent decades building the National Rally into a viable political force, softening its image, distancing it from her father's overt racism, and positioning it as a nationalist-conservative alternative rather than a neo-fascist fringe. Bardella inherited all of that work without having done any of it. He is the beneficiary of a rebranding he did not engineer, running for an office his mentor can no longer contest, on a platform he did not develop, with a life experience that bears no resemblance to the voters he courts.

---

## What This Means

France has had inexperienced leaders before. Emmanuel Macron was thirty-nine when he became president, and his pre-political career — investment banking at Rothschild — drew its own criticism. But Macron had at least worked in the private economy, managed teams, negotiated deals, and reported to shareholders. He had done something outside the state.

Bardella has not. His entire adult life — from age sixteen to thirty — has been spent inside one political party, rising through ranks created for him by a patron who is now legally barred from the office he seeks. He knows party congresses, EU committee rooms, and television studios. He does not know a payroll, a profit-and-loss statement, a factory floor, a hospital ward, or a classroom of his own.

This is not an argument that politicians must have been manual labourers. It is an observation that Bardella's specific claim — that he understands the struggles of ordinary French people because he lived them — does not survive scrutiny. He lived in a flat block, yes, but he was educated privately, spent weekends in a wealthy suburb, and entered politics before he could legally buy alcohol. He has never paid rent with a non-political salary. He has never been fired. He has never managed a business that was not a party branch.

The far right in France has long accused the establishment of being out of touch with real life. The irony is that its next presidential candidate may be the most professionally insulated politician in the field. He has not been rejected by the system. He has never even applied.

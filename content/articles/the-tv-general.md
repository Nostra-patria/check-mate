# The TV General: How a Fox News Weekend Host Became America's Secretary of War

*A man who spent Saturday mornings on cable television now runs the world's most powerful military. The consequences are exactly what you would expect.*

---

**1. The Execution Order**

On the morning of 11 June 2026, the United States Secretary of Defense stood on the tarmac at Guantánamo Bay, looked at the journalists assembled in the Florida sun, and said the quiet part out loud.

"My personal opinion is that the prisoners in Guantánamo should have been executed long ago for their crimes against the American people."

The man who said this was Pete Hegseth, the Secretary of Defense of the United States. He was not speaking hypothetically. He was not answering a trick question. He had just returned from a visit to the military prison where he himself had served as a guard twenty years earlier, and he was announcing, as the sitting head of the most powerful military on Earth, that fifteen men held without conviction — including some never charged with any crime — should simply be killed.

He blamed the delay on the usual suspects: "international activist groups, human rights organisations, and lawyers." The machinery of American justice, he said, had been "clogged" since his time as a guard in 2004 and 2005. The solution, apparently, was to skip the trial and go straight to the firing squad.

Of the fifteen men still detained at Guantánamo, only two have been convicted by military commission. Seven cases are still pending — some after two decades of pre-trial detention. Three are held indefinitely without charge or trial. Three others are eligible for transfer but remain in limbo. Among the unconvicted is Khalid Sheikh Mohammed, the alleged architect of the September 11 attacks, whose military commission proceedings have dragged on since 2008 without resolution, in part because evidence obtained through waterboarding and other forms of torture is inadmissible in any legitimate court.

None of this complexity made it into Hegseth's statement. What emerged instead was a single, uncomplicated demand: kill them. The Secretary of Defense of the United States had just called for the extrajudicial execution of detainees under his authority, in a facility that has become an international symbol of American lawlessness.

It was, in other words, a perfect Hegseth moment.

---

**2. The Weekend Host**

Pete Hegseth was not a general. He was not a Pentagon bureaucrat. He was not a diplomat or a strategist or a congressional veteran with decades of Armed Services Committee hearings behind him. He was the co-host of *Fox & Friends Weekend*, a cable news programme whose typical Saturday morning segment might involve grilling techniques, culture-war grievances, and friendly banter about the president's golf game.

When Donald Trump nominated him for Secretary of Defense in November 2024, the reaction in Washington was not surprise — it was exhaustion. Trump had already spent four years turning the federal bureaucracy into a television studio. Hegseth was simply the most literal expression of the trend: a man whose primary qualification was that he looked comfortable on camera, said the right things about "the troops," and had spent a decade on Fox News telling viewers what they wanted to hear about American greatness.

His military record was real, if thin. He had served in the Army National Guard, deployed to Iraq and Afghanistan, earned a Bronze Star and a Combat Infantryman's Badge. He had been a guard at Guantánamo Bay in 2004 and 2005, during the darkest years of the prison's operation, when detainees were subjected to "enhanced interrogation techniques" that the CIA's own internal review later admitted were torture. He had run Concerned Veterans for America, a Koch-funded advocacy group that lobbied for privatising veterans' health care.

None of this added up to Secretary of Defense material. But the Trump administration was not looking for material. It was looking for loyalty, camera presence, and a willingness to treat the Pentagon as an extension of the president's political messaging operation.

Hegseth sailed through his confirmation hearing in January 2025 with a promise to foster a "warrior culture" at the Defense Department — a phrase that sounded motivational in a Fox News segment and ominous when applied to the organisation responsible for nuclear launch codes. He faced allegations of sexual assault from a 2017 incident in Monterey, California, which he denied and for which no charges were filed. He faced questions about excessive drinking, to which he responded by vowing not to drink if confirmed. He faced scrutiny over financial mismanagement at the veterans' nonprofits he had led.

The Senate vote was 50-50. Vice President JD Vance broke the tie. Hegseth was sworn in on 25 January 2025.

It was the closest confirmation vote for a Defense Secretary in modern American history.

---

**3. The Signal Leaks**

If Hegseth's appointment suggested a man who treated national security as television, his first major scandal confirmed it.

On 24 March 2025, Jeffrey Goldberg, the editor-in-chief of *The Atlantic* magazine, published an extraordinary account: he had been accidentally added to a Signal group chat with senior Trump administration national security officials, including the Secretary of Defense, the National Security Advisor, and the Vice President. The chat contained detailed operational plans for an imminent US airstrike against Houthi targets in Yemen — timing, weapons systems, target sequencing. Goldberg received the messages roughly two hours before the bombs fell.

The White House confirmed the story was real.

Hegseth's immediate response was to deny the obvious. "Nobody was texting war plans," he told reporters, a claim so directly contradicted by the messages Goldberg had published that it required no rebuttal. The operational details had come from Hegseth's own phone. The journalist had watched the strike unfold in real time from the same encrypted chat where the Secretary of Defense had shared targeting information.

The Signal app is encrypted, but it is not approved for classified communications. It is a consumer messaging platform available to anyone with a smartphone. Using it to discuss active military operations — particularly with a journalist accidentally cc'd — would be a firing offence for a junior intelligence analyst. For the Secretary of Defense, it was merely embarrassing, or would have been in any administration that still distinguished between competence and loyalty.

The Yemen leak was not an isolated lapse. In April 2025, a second alleged Signal leak emerged, this one involving Hegseth again. By this point, the pattern was clear: the man running the US military treated operational security with the same casual disregard he had brought to weekend television segments.

---

**4. The Warrior Culture**

Hegseth's "warrior culture" has proven less about military excellence and more about personal vendetta.

In September 2025, Hegseth delivered a speech to assembled admirals and generals that, according to *The New York Times*, announced his intention to block the promotions of two female officers and two Black officers. The specific officers were not named publicly, but the message was unmistakable: the Secretary of Defense was personally intervening in military personnel decisions based on demographic criteria, in a force that had spent decades trying to build a merit-based promotion system.

By early 2026, *The Daily Beast* reported that Hegseth had axed more than two dozen senior officers across all four branches of the military, forced out a Navy secretary, and meddled in promotions at a scale unprecedented for a civilian secretary. The purge was not based on performance reviews or strategic disagreements. It was based on loyalty tests and ideological alignment — the same criteria that had governed his own appointment.

In May 2026, Hegseth abruptly cancelled the deployment of thousands of US troops to Poland, one of Washington's staunchest NATO allies, without consulting senior Pentagon officials or the White House. President Trump learned of the decision from Polish officials and reportedly "unloaded" on Hegseth in a furious phone call. Trump later overruled his own Defense Secretary and ordered the deployment reinstated.

The episode captured the Hegseth method in miniature: act first, think later, create chaos, retreat under pressure. It was the Fox News segment model applied to alliance management.

---

**5. The Return to Gitmo**

Which brings us back to Guantánamo Bay, and the statement that prompted this article.

Hegseth's visit to Gitmo in June 2026 was not his first as Secretary of Defense. He had visited in February 2025, shortly after taking office, to observe the Trump administration's expansion of migrant detention at the same facility. He had warned Cuba against acquiring weapons that could threaten the United States. He had treated the base as a political prop — a backdrop for tough-guy posturing, the same role it had served for twenty years of post-9/11 politics.

But the June statement was different. It was not posturing. It was a call for state killing without process, issued by the man constitutionally responsible for the military justice system.

The context matters. Guantánamo has held 779 men since 2002. Most were released without charge, after years of detention. Some were tortured. Many were innocent. The military commissions have produced exactly two convictions. The rest of the cases are either pending in procedural purgatory or have been abandoned entirely, because the evidence against the detainees was obtained through methods that no legitimate court would admit.

Hegseth's solution to this two-decade legal and moral failure is not to fix the process, grant fair trials, or release the innocent. It is to execute everyone and be done with it.

The language is notable for its casualness. He is not proposing a policy. He is venting an opinion — the same way he might have vented about a Democratic congressman on Saturday morning television. But he is no longer a television host. He is the Secretary of Defense, and his opinions shape the operations of a military that controls enough nuclear warheads to end human civilisation.

---

**6. What It Means**

The Pete Hegseth story is not a scandal. It is a structural feature of the Trump administration's approach to governance.

The appointment of a Fox News weekend host to run the Pentagon was not an aberration. It was the culmination of a decade-long project to replace expertise with loyalty, institutional knowledge with media performance, and strategic thinking with the instincts of a cable news segment. Hegseth was chosen precisely because he would say things like "kill the Gitmo prisoners" on camera — because he would treat the Defense Department as a communications operation, because he would purge officers who failed ideological tests, because he would share war plans in a group chat and then deny it with the same brazenness he had honed on television.

The results are predictable. An ally blindsided by a cancelled deployment. A journalist accidentally invited to a classified war-planning chat. Senior officers purged for demographic reasons. And now, a sitting Defense Secretary calling for the extrajudicial execution of prisoners under his authority.

Each of these would have ended a career in any previous administration. In this one, they are simply Tuesday.

The deeper question is not whether Hegseth is competent. He is not, and that is not in dispute. The deeper question is what happens when the world's most powerful military is run by a man who approaches the job with the same seriousness he brought to weekend television — when the distinction between cable news performance and national security policy collapses entirely, and the Secretary of Defense becomes indistinguishable from the weekend host who used to tell viewers that America was great.

The answer, apparently, is that you get an execution order.

Not from a court. Not from a jury. Not from any process the United States claims to defend. From a man in Florida, fresh from a prison visit, blaming lawyers and human rights groups for the fact that he has not yet been allowed to kill the prisoners in his charge.

It is not policy. It is a television segment. And it is running the Pentagon.

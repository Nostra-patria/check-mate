# The Import Model: How Organized Violence Gets Sold as Grassroots Protest

> **P6 Investigation** — This article originated from a P6 narrative intelligence cluster flagged as CRITICAL (20.0) on 2026-06-13. The cluster framed the Apeldoorn and Loosdrecht asylum protests as "grievance" — spontaneous citizen anger. Grounding analysis revealed the events were imported, coordinated violence by external actors. P6 has since reclassified the framing as `organized_violence`.

On 5 May 2026, a protest began in Apeldoorn against a plan to house 240 asylum seekers in a vacant school building. By the third night, it had become a riot. Protesters refused to disperse at the agreed 8 PM deadline. Police were pelted with fireworks. Traffic was blocked. A vehicle was attacked. Dozens were arrested.

The pattern repeated in Loosdrecht, where nightly confrontations with riot police stretched across weeks. A building in Den Bosch — planned to house 50 unaccompanied adolescent refugees — was hit by an explosion. The window shattered. Police found remnants of fireworks and flammable liquid.

The headlines wrote themselves: *locals revolt against asylum centers*. Politicians framed it as spontaneous civic anger. The P6 narrative intelligence system initially scored the cluster as CRITICAL with a `grievance` frame — citizens wronged by policy.

But the numbers told a different story.

## The 40% Problem

An analysis by Dutch broadcaster NOS, published on 20 May 2026, examined 163 arrests across 34 local anti-asylum protests since January 2025. Of those whose hometowns were disclosed, 61 lived locally. Forty-four did not.

That is not a margin of error. That is a structural feature.

The largest distance was covered by a 48-year-old Rotterdammer, arrested at a "noise protest" in 's-Heerenberg — 143 kilometers from his home. Another man from Emmen traveled 114 kilometers to Apeldoorn, where he joined a demonstration that included attacking a passing car.

These are not residents walking to a town hall meeting. These are people driving two hours to throw fireworks at police.

## The Organizers

Some of the outside involvement is not hidden — it is advertised.

**Defend Netherlands**, a far-right network, posted a public call on Facebook on 18 April 2026 for protesters to travel to Loosdrecht. Weeks later, nightly riots began. A local Defend chapter formed in the village. The organization now openly advocates unrest as a protest tactic.

Its representative, Eldor van Feggelen, told a municipal council meeting in Uitgeest in February 2026: "Where it did not remain calm, that is simply a fact, the municipality changed its policy." The message is unambiguous: violence works.

The international far-right network **Identitair Verzet** has also appeared at protests in Loosdrecht, IJsselstein, and Tilburg. Unlike Defend Netherlands, which operates in plain sight, Identitair Verzet brings ideological infrastructure: trained activists, coordinated messaging, and cross-border experience in disrupting public order.

The imported actors are not limited to organized political groups. Dutch mayors have identified **motorcycle clubs**, **football hooligans**, and **people from Belgium** traveling to protests in Uden, Maashorst, and elsewhere. Mayor Hans Van der Pas of Maashorst stated explicitly: "These people really don't travel for hours just for a demonstration. They come for something else — to intimidate and threaten."

## The Method: Chaos as a Commodity

The operational pattern is consistent across locations:

1. **Seed**: A local council announces an asylum facility.
2. **Call**: Far-right networks post travel instructions on social media.
3. **Import**: Actors from outside the municipality arrive — sometimes organized, sometimes self-selected.
4. **Escalate**: The imported contingent pushes the protest past its permitted parameters. Fireworks, projectiles, property damage.
5. **Reframe**: National media and politicians describe the violence as "local residents" expressing anger.
6. **Extract**: Municipalities retreat. Loosdrecht reduced its planned capacity from 110 to 70 beds. Den Bosch postponed its plans.

This is not protest. This is coercion by importation.

## The Laundering Cycle

The most insidious part is not the violence itself — it is what happens to the narrative afterward.

When a far-right politician stands before a crowd in Loosdrecht and speaks of "the people's voice," the crowd is not the people of Loosdrecht. It is a curated assembly of travelers: Rotterdammers, Emmenaren, Belgians, motorcycle club members, and organized activists. Their violence is then laundered into legitimacy.

The cycle completes when mainstream political figures cite these events as evidence of "public sentiment." Gidi Markuszower, founder of the DNA party after splitting from Geert Wilders' PVV, gave a speech at a Loosdrecht march saying refugees should "go back to their own country." FvD leader Lidewij de Vos attended, telling the press she "only supports peaceful protests" — while standing in the aftermath of nightly riots that had already forced a council to reduce refugee beds.

The violence is real. The local grievance is synthetic.

## What the AIVD Saw

The scale of outside involvement prompted Dutch intelligence service AIVD to launch a formal investigation. Asylum Minister Bart van den Brink announced on 13 May 2026 that the agency would examine whether there is "organized activity" behind the rallies.

The AIVD does not investigate spontaneous civic frustration. It investigates threats to national security. The fact that it was mobilized for municipal asylum protests — traditionally the domain of local police — is itself a signal. Something in the pattern crossed the threshold from civil disobedience to coordinated destabilization.

The investigation is ongoing. But the evidence already in the public domain — 40% imported actors, open calls to travel, cross-border participation, motorcycle clubs, football hooligans, and a stated organizational philosophy that violence achieves policy change — suggests the AIVD is not looking for something that might exist. It is looking for the full architecture of something already visible.

## Why This Matters

Democratic protest is a pressure valve. It allows citizens to register dissent without collapsing the system. But the import model breaks that contract.

When violence is imported, the local population becomes a prop. The municipality does not negotiate with its residents; it negotiates with the threat of imported chaos. The legitimate concerns of locals — about housing pressure, about integration capacity, about municipal resources — are drowned out by the smashing of windows and the throwing of fireworks.

Worse, the import model creates a **perverse incentive structure**. Every municipality that retreats after imported violence teaches the network a lesson: it works. Loosdrecht reduced its beds. Den Bosch postponed. The reward for violence is policy change.

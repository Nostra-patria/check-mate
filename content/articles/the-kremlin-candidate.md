# The Kremlin Candidate

## France is ten months from electing a president who has never worked, owes his rise to a convicted embezzler, and leads a party that repaid a Moscow loan with taxpayer money. Europe is running out of time to stop it.

In April 2027, France will vote for a new president. Emmanuel Macron, after two terms, is constitutionally barred from running again. His successor will inherit a nuclear arsenal, a permanent seat on the UN Security Council, and the second-largest economy in the European Union. According to every poll published since early 2026, that successor is likely to come from the National Rally — a party that took a €9.4 million loan from a Moscow bank tied to Vladimir Putin's inner circle, met with Putin while the debt was active, and eventually settled the balance with French public money.

The election is not a French affair. It is a European emergency hiding in plain sight.

---

## The Numbers

Jordan Bardella, the 30-year-old president of the National Rally, has polled between 34 and 38 percent in first-round scenarios since May 2026. According to French polling aggregator Élysée Scope, these are scores no candidate has reached at this stage since François Mitterrand in 1988.

In the second round, the picture tightens. A March 2026 Elabe survey showed former Prime Minister Édouard Philippe defeating Bardella by the slimmest of margins — 51.5 percent to 48.5 percent, within the margin of error. Other polls have Bardella winning second-round matchups against every challenger. The far right's historical ceiling — the glass barrier that kept Marine Le Pen from the Élysée in 2017 and 2022 — may finally have cracked.

The glass barrier itself is now in the dock.

---

## The Conviction

In March 2025, Marine Le Pen was convicted of embezzling €4.1 million in European Parliament funds and sentenced to a five-year ban on holding public office. The verdict, if upheld, would remove her from the 2027 ballot. Her appeal is expected to conclude this summer. Prosecutors have asked the court to maintain the ban. Le Pen has called the verdict a "political decision." Donald Trump, from the White House, agreed: "She was banned for running for five years, and she's the leading candidate."

If the ban stands, Bardella becomes the RN's presidential candidate by default — the heir to a party he did not build, running for an office his mentor can no longer contest. If the ban is overturned, Le Pen returns to the race she has already lost twice, and the National Rally faces a different kind of reckoning: a leader carrying a criminal conviction into a presidential campaign against a centrist field that has learned, painfully, how to unite against her.

Either scenario ends with the National Rally at the gates of power.

---

## The Résumé

Bardella's qualifications for the presidency rest on a biography that does not survive scrutiny. He has never held a job outside politics. His only recorded employment lasted five months in 2015, as a parliamentary assistant to a National Front MEP — a role that was, by definition, political. He was nineteen. He has never worked since.

He presents himself as the voice of the banlieue, the kid from the tower block who made good. The details complicate the narrative. Bardella grew up in Drancy, Seine-Saint-Denis, but attended the semi-private Catholic lycée Jean-Baptiste-de-La-Salle in Saint-Denis. His father, a small-business owner, lived in Montmorency, a wealthy suburb. Bardella spent weekends there. He failed the entrance exam for Sciences Po, enrolled in geography at Paris-Sorbonne University, and dropped out. By twenty, he was a regional councillor. By twenty-seven, he was president of the National Rally.

He has never managed a payroll, negotiated a contract, or reported to a boss who was not a party official. He has never lived outside the insulated world of EU per diems, Strasbourg expense accounts, and television studios. The far right has long accused the French establishment of being out of touch with real life. Its next presidential candidate may be the most professionally insulated politician in the field. (See: "Jordan Bardella: The Man Who Never Worked," Check Mate, 12 June 2026.)

## The Loan

The National Rally's relationship with Moscow is not theoretical. In 2014, French banks refused to lend to the Front National. The party turned east, taking a €9.4 million loan from the First Czech-Russian Bank in Moscow — a lender tied to Gennady Timchenko, a Putin associate under US sanctions. The bank collapsed in 2016. The debt was sold to a Russian car-rental firm, then to Aviazapchast, an aerospace company with links to Russian military procurement and exports to the Syrian Ministry of Defence.

In March 2017, one month before the first round of the French presidential election, Marine Le Pen sat down with Vladimir Putin in the Kremlin. Russian state media briefly published a report — later retracted — suggesting the Kremlin had announced financing for her campaign. She returned to France with photographs, talking points, and a debt still on her party's books, now held by a military-linked Russian firm.

In December 2019, Aviazapchast sued the RN in a Moscow court for roughly €11 million. The party settled in mid-2020. The debt was finally cleared in September 2023, when RN treasurer Kévin Pfeffer transferred €6,088,784 in French state party financing — taxpayer money — to close the account. Bardella, by then party president, had accelerated the repayment explicitly to "close the chapter" before the 2024 European Parliament elections.

French citizens paid, through public allocations, to scrub a Kremlin debt from a party that claims to put France first. (See: "The Kremlin Loan," Check Mate, 12 June 2026.)

## What Russia Bought

The Kremlin did not need to write policy for the National Rally. It only needed to make sure the party remembered who had extended credit when no one else would. The effects are visible in the voting record.

RN members in the European Parliament have abstained from or opposed every resolution condemning Russian aggression. According to Sciences Po foreign-policy scholar Nicolas Tenzer, the party has never voted in favour of a single pro-Ukraine or anti-Russia resolution in either the French or European Parliament. Bardella himself opposes sending French troops to Ukraine and has resisted the provision of long-range missiles to Kyiv.

The old guard has not changed. Thierry Mariani, the RN's veteran MEP, still defends Russia's occupation of Crimea and repeats the Kremlin narrative that NATO expansion triggered the war. A French defence official described the party to Politico as split into three camps: Mariani's traditional pro-Russians, a small Atlanticist faction around Bardella, and a large middle group that is "fundamentally pro-Russian" but knows better than to say so before an election.

Bardella has tried to rebrand the party's stance as "collective naïvité." He now calls Russia a "multi-dimensional threat." The receipts say otherwise.

## The European Reckoning

A National Rally presidency would not merely shift French domestic policy. It would destabilise the European order at its foundation.

**Nuclear deterrence.** The RN insists France's nuclear arsenal is strictly national and not for sharing. This directly undermines Macron's attempt to build a European nuclear umbrella — an effort that has already drawn the UK and seven non-nuclear states into talks on joint exercises and early-warning systems. Germany's chancellor, Friedrich Merz, has spoken of creating a nuclear strategy steering group with France. The RN would shut that door. Some Nordic politicians are already contemplating a "Nordic nuke" as an alternative. Poland has seen similar talk.

**The EU budget.** Bardella has vowed to halve France's net contribution to the EU budget, demanding what he calls France's "money back" — a Thatcher-style rebate that would blow a €2 to €3 billion hole in Brussels' finances and embolden every net contributor seeking similar treatment. Macron and other European leaders are racing to lock in the next seven-year budget before the election, knowing that an RN president could hold the entire framework hostage.

**Ukraine.** The RN opposes EU enlargement and has never supported Ukraine's path to membership. Under the French constitution, EU accession requires a referendum unless approved by a three-fifths majority of both parliamentary chambers — a threshold the RN could block. This is precisely why Zelenskyy and Ukraine's strongest EU supporters are pushing to secure membership terms by 2027, "to get under the wire," as Paul Taylor wrote in The Guardian, "before a potential blockade from a Le Pen- or Bardella-led Paris."

**Trade and borders.** The RN advocates trade barriers and border controls even within the European Union. A France that reinstates internal border checks, challenges free movement, and demands budget concessions would not be a reluctant partner. It would be a structural saboteur.

---

## The Guardrails

European governments are not blind to the risk. They are scrambling.

Macron has spent his final year placing loyalists in unsackable positions: Amélie de Montchalin, his 40-year-old former budget minister, as president of France's Court of Auditors; Pierre Moscovici at the European Court of Auditors. ECB president Christine Lagarde is reportedly planning to step down before her term expires in October 2027, giving Macron time to appoint the next French member of the bank's executive board for eight years. Bank of France governor François Villeroy de Galhau announced early retirement to head a charity, vacating another six-year strategic post.

"He's methodically putting guardrails in place," a senior presidential supporter told The Guardian. "All outgoing presidents have rewarded key supporters with plum jobs, but this time it's different because of the risks before us."

The risks have names. One of them is Jordan Bardella. The other is the party whose Moscow debt he buried with public money.

---

## The Countdown

Ten months remain. The National Rally leads every poll. Its candidate has never held a non-political job, never finished university, and owes his entire career to a convicted embezzler whose legal fate will be decided this summer. The party he leads repaid a Russian military-linked creditor with French taxpayer funds, then declared the matter closed.

The question is no longer whether the far right can win in France. The question is what Europe looks like when a party that owes its survival to a Kremlin loan controls a nuclear power, a Security Council veto, and the fate of the European project.

France's neighbours are already preparing for a "rogue France." The only question left is whether France itself understands what it is about to vote for.

# The Open Sky Lie

## How a Kremlin Accusation Became a Headline in the Netherlands

On June 14, 2026, the Dutch-language website FrontnieuwsCom published an article with a striking claim: **"The Baltic states and Finland have opened their airspace for Ukrainian drone attacks on Russia."**

The claim is false. Not partially false, not debatable — demonstrably, documentably false. And the path from Kremlin spokesman to Dutch headline reveals something important about how modern disinformation works: not by inventing events, but by inverting them.

---

## What Actually Happened

Between March and June 2026, a series of Ukrainian military drones entered the airspace of NATO members Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania and Finland. The drones had been launched against Russian oil export terminals at Ust-Luga and Primorsk on the Baltic Sea. Some missed their targets. Others were, according to Finnish Prime Minister Petteri Orpo, Ukrainian Foreign Minister Andrii Sybiha, and multiple NATO military analysts, **driven off course by Russian electronic jamming**.

The incidents were treated exactly as one would expect: as security emergencies. On 25 March, a drone struck the chimney of Estonia's Auvere power station. Another crashed in Latvia's Krāslava region. In Finland, an unexploded Ukrainian AN-196 warhead was discovered near Kouvola and destroyed in a controlled detonation.

The response was not cooperation. It was alarm.

Latvian Defence Minister Andris Sprūds offered his resignation after the 7 May incident, in which a drone exploded at an oil storage facility in Rēzekne. Prime Minister Evika Siliņa resigned days later, triggering the collapse of Latvia's coalition government. On 20 May, Lithuania issued its first air raid alarm since independence, sending lawmakers to underground shelters and suspending flights at Vilnius Airport. On 8 June, a French Rafale fighter jet on NATO Baltic Air Policing duty shot down a drone over eastern Latvia.

These are not the actions of countries that have "opened their airspace" for anything. These are the actions of countries under accidental — and, according to their own assessment, **Russian-engineered** — pressure.

---

## The Kremlin Frame

The inversion began with Russia.

On 31 March 2026, Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov told reporters that Russia would "respond" if other countries provided their airspace for what he called "hostile activity against Russia." The statement was a threat framed as a conditional. Peskov offered no evidence that any NATO country had done so. He did not need to. The mere suggestion was enough to plant the frame.

The frame works like this: take an event where Russia is the aggressor (electronic warfare that diverts drones into allied territory) and reposition Russia as the victim (NATO countries secretly enabling attacks). It transforms Russian jamming — a military action that caused the incidents — into evidence of NATO collusion.

It is, in essence, a confession dressed as an accusation. Russia has the technical capacity to interfere with Ukrainian drone navigation. The drones appear in NATO airspace. Russia then claims this proves NATO is helping Ukraine attack Russia. The logic is circular, but the propaganda is effective.

---

## The Baltic Response

The Baltic governments saw the frame coming. On 10 April 2026, the foreign ministers of Estonia, Latvia and Lithuania issued a joint statement that did not mince words.

"The claims are part of a Russian disinformation campaign," they wrote. "The Baltic states have formally refuted the allegations to Russian diplomatic representatives."

They had already done so in late March. The statement was not defensive — it was preemptive. They knew the accusation would migrate from Kremlin briefings to sympathetic outlets, and they wanted a rebuttal on record.

It did not stop the migration.

---

## The Pipeline

FrontnieuwsCom is not an independent news source. It is a Dutch-language outlet in the pro-Kremlin media ecosystem, known for amplifying Russian foreign policy narratives with minimal editorial distance. The 14 June article does not cite the Baltic foreign ministers. It does not cite Orpo's assessment of Russian jamming. It does not cite the Reuters, BBC, or Independent reporting that documented the accidental nature of the incursions. It simply repeats the inverted frame: NATO opened its airspace.

The article's headline is not a summary of events. It is a delivery mechanism. It takes a Russian accusation that was already refuted by the accused parties and presents it as settled fact to a Dutch-speaking audience that may not follow Baltic politics closely enough to know better.

This is the final stage of the disinformation pipeline: the laundering of a political threat into a news headline.

---

## Why This Matters

The Open Sky Lie is not about Ukraine, or the Baltics, or Finland. It is about **credibility architecture** — the systematic erosion of what audiences believe they can trust.

If a NATO country defends itself against a stray drone, and the story is reported as "NATO opens airspace for attacks on Russia," then no amount of official rebuttal matters. The frame has already done its work. Readers who see the Frontnieuws headline do not need to believe it entirely. They only need to believe that "something is going on," that "the mainstream media isn't telling the whole story," that "there are two sides."

There are not two sides to this. There is a documented series of accidents, a documented Russian disinformation campaign, and a documented set of official rebuttals. The "other side" is a Kremlin spokesman's threat and a Dutch website's willingness to repeat it as news.

The Baltic states did not open their airspace. They scrambled NATO fighter jets, issued air raid warnings, and lost a government over the stress of trying to keep it closed. That is what happened. Everything else is the lie.

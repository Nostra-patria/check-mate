# The Dutch Pincer: How Forum voor Democratie Hollows Out the EU From Within

*A party that says it wants to leave the European Union has figured out something better: staying inside, and breaking it.*

---

**1. The Paradox at the Podium**

In March 2024, Forum voor Democratie's only member of the European Parliament stood up in Strasbourg and told the chamber that Ukraine was running "child kennels for paedophile networks." He voted against the resolution condemning Russia's invasion. He voted against the resolution calling Russia a state sponsor of terrorism. He was, by every measurable standard, doing the Kremlin's work from inside the legislature sworn to resist it.

His name is Marcel de Graaff. He is not a rogue operator. He is Forum voor Democratie's official representative in Brussels.

This is the Dutch paradox of Europe's far-right insurgency. Forum voor Democratie still officially campaigns for "Nexit" — the Netherlands leaving the European Union. Its founder Thierry Baudet once described the exit procedure as simple: "Article 50, a letter, best EU, fuck you, greetings Netherlands." But Nexit polls badly in the Netherlands, and Baudet is smarter than the Brexit crowd. He has found something more effective than leaving: staying, and hollowing the institution out from within.

It is the same strategy Marine Le Pen's Rassemblement National has pursued in France — abandoning Frexit for "sovereignty from within," using parliamentary seats not to build but to freeze. But the Dutch case carries a distinction the French cannot match: a documented, on-the-record pipeline to Russian intelligence that runs from a Kremlin-linked operative through WhatsApp messages to the party's founder, through an ideologue on a Moscow payroll, and into the European Parliament via an employee who was raided by Belgian police for suspected Russian influence operations.

Forum voor Democratie is not merely a Eurosceptic party. It is a node in a cross-border influence network that operates inside the EU institutions it claims to despise.

---

**2. The Kornilov Pipeline**

The documented story begins in the autumn of 2015, when Thierry Baudet, then a 32-year-old academic and commentator, met Vladimir Kornilov for an interview on a Russian website.

Kornilov was not an ordinary journalist. Born in Russia, he had served from 2004 to 2013 as director of the Institute of CIS Countries in Kyiv — an organisation headquartered in Moscow that, according to Michael Carpenter, then the Pentagon's director for Russia and a US National Security Council official, reported directly to the Kremlin on Ukrainian affairs. "The goal is to plead for an alliance with Russia and to weaken Western societies and institutions like NATO and the EU," Carpenter told Dutch investigative programme Zembla. "They use propaganda and incitement."

Baudet knew what Kornilov was. In internal Forum voor Democratie WhatsApp messages — leaked to Zembla and confirmed by multiple party insiders including former board member Henk Otten — Baudet described Kornilov to colleagues as "een Rus die werkt voor Poetin." A Russian who works for Putin.

What followed was not a passing acquaintance. Baudet and Kornilov met repeatedly. Baudet translated at least two of Kornilov's pieces into Dutch for publication on Dutch news sites TPO and GeenStijl. One of those articles was later described by NRC Handelsblad as "a direct Russian influence operation." Baudet himself told Zembla he was aware that Kornilov had Kremlin connections — "a suspicion," he called it — but defended his actions as giving "the other side" a voice.

The app messages reveal something more specific. In November 2015, Baudet announced he was stopping as a paid commentator for Dutch television. He wrote to a colleague: "misschien wil Kornilov wel extra betalen" — maybe Kornilov wants to pay extra — followed by a winking emoji. Later, thanking a colleague for moral support, he wrote: "Daar kan geen Kornilov met al z'n geld tegenop" — No Kornilov with all his money can compete with that — followed by a sunglasses emoji.

Baudet's defence, offered to Zembla four years later, was that these were "ironic" jokes, part of a "running gag" about Russian influence. Otten, who saw the messages at the time, did not read them as jokes. He warned Baudet repeatedly about Russian influence, though he told Zembla he never saw payments flow through the party treasury.

The payment question remains technically unresolved. What is not unresolved is the pipeline itself: a Dutch party founder translating Kremlin-linked content, describing his source as "a Russian who works for Putin," and appointing as party ideologue a man — John Laughland — who worked for a Russia-financed think tank in Paris and described the European Union as "a kind of Nazi ideology, a war project."

Carpenter, the Pentagon official, put a broader frame on it. "This was the first time we saw clear Russian interference to influence the outcome of a referendum," he said, referring to the 2016 Ukraine association agreement referendum in which Baudet campaigned for "Nee." "We didn't realise at the time that this was the beginning of a movement we saw in US elections, Brexit, the Italian referendum."

The Dutch referendum was the test case. Forum voor Democratie was the vehicle.

**3. The Operator in Brussels**

If Baudet built the pipeline, Marcel de Graaff is the man standing at the European Parliament spigot.

De Graaff was originally elected for Geert Wilders's PVV but switched to Forum voor Democratie in 2022. In Brussels, he has been remarkably consistent: every vote that would strengthen the European response to Russia, he has opposed. He voted against condemning the invasion. He voted against designating Russia a state sponsor of terrorism. In speeches, he has claimed that Russia was provoked by NATO expansion and that Ukraine runs paedophile networks.

Behind de Graaff stands Guillaume P., a French national who served as his parliamentary assistant. P. is a career far-right operative who previously worked for Marine Le Pen's Rassemblement National — until he was expelled in 2019 over a photograph in which he was dressed as an Orthodox Jew. He then became assistant to Maximilian Krah, the AfD Europarliamentarian who would later be engulfed in an espionage scandal and who made public statements defending SS units.

In May 2024, Belgian police conducted three raids on properties linked to Guillaume P. — a house in Brussels and offices in Brussels and Strasbourg — at the direction of the Belgian federal prosecutor. The prosecutor announced the investigation was into "interference, passive corruption and membership of a criminal organisation." The statement was explicit: there were "indications of Russian interference, whereby members of the European Parliament would have been approached and paid for the promotion of Russian propaganda." P., the prosecutor said, "played a considerable role."

The raids were connected to the Czech intelligence service's earlier dismantling of Voice of Europe, a pro-Russian media outlet identified as a Kremlin disinformation network targeting the European elections. De Graaff's name appeared on Voice of Europe. The network's aim, according to the Czechs, was to use allied politicians across Europe to "question the territorial integrity, sovereignty and freedom of Ukraine."

When the Dutch parliament held a debate on Russian interference, Baudet — by then a member of parliament — did not attend. He called it "fact-free talk about rumours and insinuations." On social media, he declared: "There is NO money paid to us, there is NO kompromat, no blackmail, no cash, no crypto, no transfer, there is also NO other link from FvD with the Kremlin."

The declaration is notable for its absolutism. It does not address Kornilov. It does not address Laughland. It does not address de Graaff's voting record, or the Belgian raids on his assistant, or the Voice of Europe listing. It simply says: nothing to see here.

**4. The New Varnish**

In the summer of 2025, Baudet stepped back as Forum voor Democratie leader. His replacement, Lidewij de Vos, was packaged as a different kind of politician: a 28-year-old neuroscientist, articulate, female, the face of a "modernised" far-right. The varnish is thin.

In an interview with De Volkskrant, de Vos was asked whether Vladimir Putin is a threat. Her answer: "I think the current situation is a threat to Dutch security, but that comes from both sides. Russia does not want Ukraine to become an EU and NATO member. The West keeps trying to make that happen. Then you provoke Russia."

She wants all sanctions against Russia lifted. She wants all financial and military support to Ukraine stopped. Asked whether Russia would then simply occupy all of Ukraine, she replied: "If you keep giving money, the war keeps going. We don't want that. I see no other route to lasting peace."

The framing is identical to de Graaff's and to the Kremlin's: Russia as the provoked party, the West as the aggressor, peace as synonymous with Ukrainian surrender. The only difference is that de Vos delivers it in the measured tone of a scientist rather than the theatrical register of a Eurosceptic firebrand.

De Vos's moderation is cosmetic. She defended the inclusion of Freek Jansen — convicted for incitement, including a reference to "a swarm of negroid primates" in parliament — as the party's number three. She attended the remigration conference organised by Martin Sellner, the Austrian extremist convicted for putting swastika stickers on a synagogue and banned from the United States for receiving money from the Christchurch terrorist. Her partner, Massimo Etalle, interviewed Sellner for the party's youth magazine and was photographed laughing with him. When Baudet called in 2024 for "mass remigration" to maintain "a white Europe," de Vos's defence was semantic: "Remigration is a term that is simply in the dictionary."

Baudet has been equally explicit on race. In January 2024, while launching Forum voor Democratie's Flemish branch, he defended South African apartheid as a system that had simply become impossible to sustain — adding, according to reports from the event, that this was "unfortunate." He lamented that South Africa would soon no longer have enough white people, which meant, he said, that people in that part of the world would no longer be able to enjoy "the light of us." The remarks were reported by Dutch public broadcaster BNNVARA. Baudet offered no retraction. The party offered no distance. For a movement that claims to defend Dutch sovereignty against "Brussels diktat," its founder's nostalgia for white minority rule sits uncomfortably close to the Kremlin-aligned narratives he has spent years translating and amplifying.

The same Dutch intelligence service that warned about Russian influence in 2016 — the AIVD — has since warned that terms like "remigration" are being mainstreamed by extremists with deportation plans. The National Coordinator for Counterterrorism and Security (NCTV) wrote in a report that "the ever-further embrace by politicians and large groups in society of terms like 'remigration', which are used by right-wing extremists as a description of their deportation plans, is concerning."

De Vos's response: "We have no deportation plans."

It is the same manoeuvre Baudet perfected: say enough to signal alignment, then retreat behind plausible deniability.

**5. The Human Bridge**

The most revealing figure in this story may be Guillaume P., the man who moved from Marine Le Pen's Rassemblement National to Maximilian Krah's AfD to Marcel de Graaff's Forum voor Democratie. He is not a politician. He is infrastructure — the administrative staffer who holds the bridge between three Kremlin-linked parties across three countries.

The rotation is not coincidental. It reflects the operating model of Russian influence in Europe: not a single grand conspiracy but a network of interoperable parts, each knowing enough to be useful and little enough to maintain deniability. The same parties share personnel, narratives, and voting patterns. The same themes — NATO provocation, Ukrainian corruption, sanctions as self-harm — migrate from Moscow to Brussels to The Hague via translated articles, shared staffers, and synchronised parliamentary obstruction.

Forum voor Democratie fits this model precisely. It is not the largest far-right party in Europe. It is not the oldest. But it is arguably the most documented case of a direct Kremlin-to-party pipeline, from Kornilov's Moscow-linked institute through Baudet's WhatsApp to de Graaff's obstruction in Strasbourg and the Belgian raids on his assistant.

The French Rassemblement National has the €9.4 million loan from a Russian military-linked bank. The German AfD has the espionage scandals and the Voice of Europe connections. The Dutch Forum voor Democratie has the apps.

**6. The Strategy: Freeze, Don't Flee**

Baudet still says he wants Nexit. But Nexit is electorally toxic in the Netherlands, where trade with the EU accounts for the majority of exports and where the Brexit debacle across the North Sea serves as a permanent cautionary tale. Baudet knows this. The smarter play is not to leave, but to stay and sabotage.

This is the "sovereignty from within" strategy that Marine Le Pen's Rassemblement National adopted years earlier: abandon the explosive demand for exit, and instead use institutional presence to paralyse. Vote against every resolution that would strengthen European capacity. Frame every EU initiative as "Brussels diktat." Amplify every Kremlin-aligned narrative about Ukrainian corruption and NATO provocation. Turn the European Parliament into a platform for the very forces that want the European project to fail.

The result is a structural paradox. Forum voor Democratie claims to defend Dutch sovereignty. But its actual effect in Brussels is to advance Russian strategic objectives: a fractured Europe, a paralysed security policy, a Ukraine left to fend for itself. Dutch voters who support the party because they want less immigration or lower taxes are, via their MEP's voting record, supporting a foreign policy platform authored in Moscow.

It is a laundering cycle. The Kremlin seeds narratives through networks like Voice of Europe. Those narratives are amplified by sympathetic politicians in the European Parliament. The politicians cite "public concern" and "the other side of the story." The original Russian source vanishes. The synthetic grievance enters the political bloodstream as legitimate debate.

Forum voor Democratie is not merely participating in this cycle. It is a documented node in its architecture.

---

**7. What It Means**

The question is not whether Thierry Baudet received a direct payment from Vladimir Kornilov. The payment question is a distraction — the kind of binary that lets a party say "prove it" and declare itself exonerated when the paper trail stops at a boundary.

The real question is structural. Why does a Dutch party opposed to the European Union staff its European Parliament delegation with assistants who rotate between Le Pen's RN and the AfD's Krah? Why does its founder describe his Russian contact as "a Russian who works for Putin" and then translate Kremlin-aligned content for Dutch audiences? Why does its leader call for Article 50 while its MEP votes in Strasbourg to advance Moscow's foreign policy objectives? Why does its new "modern" leader adopt the identical framing — Russia as provoked, sanctions as self-harm, peace as Ukrainian surrender?

The answer is that Forum voor Democratie is not trying to reform the European Union, nor to lead the Netherlands out of it. It is trying to break it from inside — to turn the world's most successful experiment in pooled sovereignty into a hollowed-out trade zone too weak to resist bilateral pressure, too divided to defend its members, and too paralysed to notice that one of its own delegations is functioning as a transmission belt for the Kremlin.

The Dutch pincer is smaller than the French one. It has fewer seats, less money, and less institutional weight. But it may be the most transparent. Where Le Pen's Kremlin loan required years of financial investigation to document, Baudet's pipeline was right there in the apps — "a Russian who works for Putin," "maybe Kornilov wants to pay extra," "no Kornilov with all his money."

He called it irony. The Kremlin, presumably, does not.

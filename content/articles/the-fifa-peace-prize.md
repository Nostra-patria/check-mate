# The FIFA Peace Prize

## A Trophy Invented for a President Who Couldn't Win the Nobel

On the evening of 5 December 2025, inside the John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts in Washington, D.C., FIFA President Gianni Infantino walked on stage and presented Donald Trump with a large gold trophy, a certificate, and what he described as "a beautiful medal for you, you can wear everywhere you go." The award was the inaugural "FIFA Peace Prize — Football Unites the World." Trump put the medal around his neck and called it "one of the great honours of my life."

What Infantino did not mention was that the prize had not existed three weeks earlier. It was announced in early November 2025, roughly twenty-one days after the Nobel Committee awarded its Peace Prize to Venezuelan democracy campaigner María Corina Machado — bypassing Trump, who had publicly campaigned for it. The prize had no selection criteria, no named candidates, no judges, and no discussion with the FIFA Council or vice presidents, several of whom were reportedly surprised by its creation. Human Rights Watch sent Infantino a formal letter asking who the nominees were, who judged them, and what qualifications those judges held. FIFA never replied. Months later, FIFA has still not published formal selection criteria, a jury list, or nominees.

If the prize looked like a consolation trophy for a president who could not win a real one, that is because it was.

The irony has only sharpened since. Months after the ceremony, María Corina Machado — the actual recipient of the 2025 Nobel Peace Prize — presented her medal to Trump in a gesture of gratitude for his perceived support of Venezuela. Even when a real Nobel laureate gives her prize to the man Infantino tried to honour, the FIFA trophy does not look more credible. It looks cheaper.

---

## The Courtship

Infantino's enthusiasm for Trump is not a recent development. In 2025 alone, he visited Washington approximately eight times, five of them in the Oval Office. FIFA opened an office in Trump Tower, New York. In August 2025, Infantino sat beside Trump in the Oval Office while the president promoted his immigration crackdown. When Trump asked for approval, Infantino gave a solemn "oh yes." He then presented Trump with a ceremonial ticket to the World Cup final — Row 1, Seat 1. Politico noted that Infantino had "logged more face time with Trump this year than any world leader."

This is the same FIFA president whose organization's Statutes, in Article 3, state: "FIFA is committed to respecting all internationally recognized human rights and shall strive to promote the protection of these rights." Article 4 adds "non-discrimination, equality and neutrality." Infantino himself pledged in 2016, after the Harvard Ruggie report, that FIFA would "meet its responsibilities under the United Nations Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights." In April 2016, FIFA adopted those UN principles.

The gap between those commitments and Infantino's conduct is now wide enough to host a World Cup in.

---

## The Hypocrisy

Consider what the Peace Prize was awarded for. FIFA's official press release praised Trump for his "tireless efforts to promote peace," citing his role in a ceasefire between Israel and Palestine and "actively seeking to end other conflicts." But the Trump administration had already renamed the Defense Department the "Department of War." The administration had reportedly ordered drone bombings of alleged drug smugglers. PolitiFact rated his claim that he "ended seven unendable wars" as "mostly false" — a figure that fluctuated between six and eight depending on the speech. The Nation described him at the time as "a president inflicting violence both at home and abroad."

Within a month of receiving the prize, the absurdity deepened. In January 2026, U.S. troops invaded Venezuela and removed President Nicolás Maduro. Fatal shootings of American citizens occurred amid an immigration crackdown in Minneapolis. By the time Infantino defended the prize on Sky News two months later, the U.S. had already invaded a sovereign nation and conducted deportation raids — precisely the period for which the prize had supposedly been awarded. His response: "Whatever we can do to help peace in the world, we should be doing it... objectively, he deserves it."

Objectively.

The prize is only the most theatrical example of a broader pattern. In February 2025, Infantino defended FIFA's decision to award the 2034 World Cup to Saudi Arabia, a kingdom where rights groups have documented preventable deaths of migrant workers on construction sites. He defended that decision and his Trump relationship in the same breath. In April 2026, FIFA fined the Israeli Football Association roughly $191,000 for crowd behavior while scheduling an Iran vs. Egypt match in Seattle — Iran being a country where homosexuality is punishable by death. Two World Cup qualifiers, Iran and Haiti, are subject to Trump's travel bans, yet Infantino insists they will play in the United States because, as he repeats at every opportunity, "football unites the world."

The slogan is now so threadbare that The Guardian's Football Daily called the prize "The Pretend Peace Prize." Play the Game observed in 2022 that "football may unite the world, but FIFA works to divide it." In December 2025, Human Rights Watch noted that FIFA had "obsequiously cancelled its own anti-racism and anti-discrimination campaigns" in the lead-up to the Trump ceremony.

## The Enrichment

While Infantino preaches unity, he has done rather well for himself. In March 2026, FIFA disclosed that his total compensation for 2025 reached approximately $6 million — a base salary of around $3.3 million plus a bonus of roughly $2.78 million, a 33% increase. Over his ten years as president, his pay has quadrupled. He once described FIFA's compensation committee as "insulting" and "completely arbitrary." Now it appears to be working in his favor.

There are other curiosities. In December 2025, Politico reported that Infantino "launched a crypto coin in the Oval Office." FIFA decided in 2024 that his name would be engraved on the 2025 Club World Cup trophy, which bears the inscription: "We are witness to a new age." He has partnered with Trump's "Board of Peace" and presented an AI-generated video of a $75 million "football ecosystem" for Gaza while, according to The New Yorker, rocking out as Argentine President Javier Milei sang along to an Elvis song.

This is not the behavior of a sports administrator. It is the behavior of a courtier.

---

## The Lonely Dissenter

Inside FIFA, there has been dissent. Senior officials were blindsided by the Peace Prize. The Globe and Mail and AP reported that ethics complaints were filed against Infantino in December 2025. Politico cited FIFA insiders who believe Infantino "has catered too much to Trump for a tournament realized through the cooperation of three nations."

But only one national federation president has spoken publicly. Lise Klaveness of Norway stood up two months after the ceremony and described what it felt like to sit in that Kennedy Center room: "I felt the painful feeling of being hostage to something that is clearly wrong. The feeling that the emperor is not only walking without clothes — but that he is leading us in a dangerous direction, and that, at the same time, I can't stop it."

She has been the exception. Everyone else, as The New Yorker put it, "takes the magic ball and smiles." Even Sepp Blatter — the former FIFA president ousted in a corruption scandal — has criticized the 2026 format, which is perhaps the most damning indictment of all: when Blatter is the voice of institutional caution, something has gone very wrong.

---

## What This Means

The FIFA Peace Prize was not an award. It was a transaction. Infantino needed Trump's goodwill for the 2026 World Cup, which will be the most profitable sporting event in history and will flow most of its revenue back to FIFA headquarters in Switzerland. Trump needed validation after losing the Nobel. The trophy — sculpted by Azerbaijani artists and presented without process — gave both men what they wanted.

What it did not give was anything to the values FIFA claims to hold. The organization that says it protects human rights gave its highest honor to a president whose administration was, at that very moment, conducting deportation raids, travel bans, and military invasions. The organization that says "football unites the world" scheduled matches in countries where its own fans may be arrested for who they are. The president who says he works for the global game spent more time in the Oval Office in 2025 than in most FIFA member nations.

The prize has already become, as Gavin Newsom's press office mocked, a "participation trophy." But participation in what? Not peace. Not football. Just power. And Gianni Infantino has made clear which side he is on.
